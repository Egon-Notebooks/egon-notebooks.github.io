- Feeling like a fraud — as though your achievements are undeserved and it's only a matter of time before others find out — is an experience many people recognise. This is often called imposter syndrome, and it is far more common than most people realise. It can show up in workplaces, academic settings, creative pursuits, and even in personal relationships, quietly undermining a person's confidence despite clear evidence of their ability and effort.
- One thing that can help is recognising that imposter feelings are thoughts, not facts. Our minds can be skilled at amplifying self-doubt while minimising genuine accomplishments. Many people find it useful to keep a simple record of their achievements, positive feedback they have received, or moments when they handled something well. Returning to this record during moments of self-doubt can offer a more balanced perspective than the one anxiety tends to provide.
- Talking openly about these feelings is another step that many people find meaningful. Imposter syndrome often thrives in silence, sustained by the belief that everyone else feels confident and capable. When people share their experiences, they frequently discover that colleagues, friends, or peers feel exactly the same way. This sense of shared humanity can reduce the shame and isolation that imposter feelings can bring.
- It can also be worth gently challenging the standards being set. Many people who experience imposter syndrome hold themselves to exceptionally high expectations, viewing anything short of perfection as failure. Reflecting on whether those standards would be applied to someone else — and whether they are truly fair — can gradually shift this pattern of thinking.
- Imposter syndrome does not reflect the truth of who someone is or what they are capable of. If these feelings are persistent or are significantly affecting daily life, speaking with a mental health professional can offer real support and help a person build a more compassionate relationship with themselves.
