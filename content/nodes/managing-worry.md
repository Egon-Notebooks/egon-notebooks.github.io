- Worry is something every person experiences at some point — it is a natural part of being human. But when worry becomes persistent or overwhelming, it can start to affect daily life, relationships, and overall wellbeing. The good news is that there are practical, evidence-informed approaches that many people find genuinely helpful in managing worry more effectively.
- One strategy that many people find useful is setting aside a specific, limited time each day to focus on their worries — sometimes called a "worry period." Rather than trying to push anxious thoughts away entirely, this approach involves acknowledging them at a set time and gently redirecting attention when they arise outside of that window. Over time, this can help reduce the sense that worry is constant or uncontrollable.
- Another approach is learning to distinguish between worries that are within your control and those that are not. When a worry is actionable, taking even a small step toward addressing it can ease the sense of helplessness that often accompanies anxiety. When a worry falls outside your control, practising acceptance — acknowledging the uncertainty without trying to resolve it — can help reduce its grip. Mindfulness-based techniques, such as slow, deliberate breathing or grounding exercises, may also support this process by helping to bring attention back to the present moment.
- Staying connected with others is also something many people find meaningful. Sharing concerns with a trusted friend, family member, or colleague can reduce the isolation that worry can sometimes create, and an outside perspective may offer a sense of reassurance.
- If worry is significantly affecting your day-to-day life, it is worth knowing that support is available. Speaking with a healthcare professional or trained therapist is a positive step — not a sign of weakness — and many people find that with the right support, things can genuinely improve.
