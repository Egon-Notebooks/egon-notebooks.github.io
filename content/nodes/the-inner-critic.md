- Many people are familiar with that persistent internal voice that finds fault, second-guesses decisions, or whispers that they are not good enough. This voice is often called the inner critic, and while it can feel overwhelming at times, it is a common part of the human experience — not a sign that something is fundamentally wrong.
- The inner critic often develops early in life, shaped by our experiences, relationships, and the messages we absorbed from the world around us. In small doses, a self-reflective voice can actually be useful — helping us learn from mistakes or motivate ourselves to grow. The difficulty arises when that voice becomes harsh, relentless, or starts to feel like the whole truth about who we are. When this happens, it can affect confidence, relationships, and overall wellbeing.
- One approach many people find helpful is simply noticing the inner critic rather than automatically believing it. Treating it as a voice rather than a fact creates a little distance, which can make it feel less powerful. Some people find it useful to ask: "Would I speak to a friend this way?" Often, the answer is no — and that awareness alone can be a starting point for responding to ourselves with more kindness.
- Practising self-compassion is another strategy that has growing evidence behind it. This does not mean ignoring genuine mistakes or avoiding accountability. Rather, it means acknowledging difficulties with the same warmth and understanding we might offer someone we care about. Over time, many people find this gradually shifts the tone of their internal world.
- If the inner critic feels loud, constant, or is significantly affecting daily life, it can help to talk things through with someone you trust, or to explore support from a mental health professional. With the right help, many people find ways to quieten that critical voice and relate to themselves with greater compassion.
