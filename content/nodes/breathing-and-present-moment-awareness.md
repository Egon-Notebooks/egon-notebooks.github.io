- Breathing is something the body does automatically, moment to moment, without us having to think about it. Yet when we bring our conscious attention to it, the breath becomes one of the most accessible tools we have for settling the nervous system and gently returning the mind to the present moment. For many people, this simple shift in awareness can feel like a quiet anchor in the middle of a busy or overwhelming day.
- One approach that many people find helpful is slow, deliberate breathing — for example, inhaling gently for a count of four, pausing briefly, and exhaling slowly for a count of six or more. Extending the out-breath in this way is thought to activate the body's natural calming response, helping to ease the physical sensations that often accompany stress or anxiety. It does not require any special equipment or setting, and even a few mindful breaths can create a small but meaningful pause.
- Present-moment awareness — sometimes called mindfulness — works alongside breathwork by gently redirecting attention away from worrying thoughts about the past or future and back to what is happening right now. Noticing the physical sensation of breathing, the rise and fall of the chest, or the feeling of air moving in and out can help interrupt cycles of anxious thinking. Over time, practising this kind of attention, even for short periods each day, may help build a greater sense of steadiness and emotional resilience.
- It is worth remembering that these practices are not about achieving a perfectly calm or clear mind — thoughts will still arise, and that is entirely normal. The intention is simply to practise returning, again and again, to the present moment. For anyone finding it difficult to manage their mental wellbeing, speaking with a healthcare professional or trained counsellor is a meaningful and worthwhile step to take.
