- How a person feels about their body can have a profound effect on their overall wellbeing. Body image — the thoughts, feelings, and perceptions we hold about our physical appearance — is shaped by many things over time, including cultural messages, social media, personal experiences, and the comments of people around us. For many people, these influences can make it genuinely difficult to feel at ease in their own skin, and that difficulty is far more common than it might seem.
- One approach that many people find helpful is practising a shift in focus — from how the body looks to what the body does. Noticing the ways your body carries you through the day, allows you to connect with others, or helps you experience the world can gently begin to reframe your relationship with it. This is not about forcing positivity, but about gradually widening the lens through which you see yourself.
- Another strategy that research supports is becoming more aware of the media and social environments that influence how you feel about yourself. Many people find that limiting time spent comparing themselves to others — particularly on social media — and actively seeking out diverse, realistic representations of bodies can reduce feelings of inadequacy over time. Small, consistent changes to your environment can make a meaningful difference.
- Practising self-compassion is also widely recognised as valuable. This means treating yourself with the same kindness and understanding you might offer a close friend who was struggling. Self-acceptance does not require loving everything about yourself — it simply means allowing yourself to be human, imperfect, and still worthy of care.
- If difficult feelings about your body are affecting your daily life, relationships, or mental health, reaching out to a trusted professional can be a meaningful first step. Support is available, and many people find that, with the right help, their relationship with themselves can change in ways they did not expect.
