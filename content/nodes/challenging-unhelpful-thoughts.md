- Thoughts have a powerful influence on how we feel and how we behave. When we're going through a difficult time, it's common for our minds to generate thoughts that feel completely true but may not reflect the full picture — things like "I always get everything wrong" or "no one really cares about me." Learning to gently question these kinds of thoughts is a skill that many people find genuinely helpful, and one that can be developed with practice over time.
- One approach that many people find useful is simply noticing a thought before accepting it as fact. When a distressing thought arises, it can help to pause and ask: "Is this thought based on evidence, or is my mind jumping to conclusions?" This small moment of reflection can create a little distance between you and the thought, making it feel less overwhelming. It doesn't mean dismissing your feelings — it means looking at them with a degree of curiosity rather than certainty.
- Another helpful strategy involves considering alternative perspectives. If a close friend came to you with the same thought about themselves, what might you say to them? Most of us are far kinder to others than we are to ourselves. Practising that same compassionate reasoning toward your own experiences can gently shift the way unhelpful thought patterns take hold.
- It also helps to keep in mind that thoughts are not facts, and they are not permanent. The mind can be trained — slowly, with patience — to recognise patterns that aren't serving us and to respond to them differently. This is at the heart of approaches like cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), which is a structured, evidence-based form of talking therapy that focuses on the connection between thoughts, feelings, and behaviour.
- If unhelpful thinking patterns are getting in the way of daily life, speaking with a mental health professional can make a real difference. Support is available, and things can change.
