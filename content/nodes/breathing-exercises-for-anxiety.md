- Anxiety can show up in many ways — a racing heart, shallow breathing, a mind that won't slow down. While these feelings can be overwhelming, the body carries a quiet but powerful tool that is always available: the breath. Simple breathing exercises won't make anxiety disappear, but many people find they help create a moment of calm in the midst of difficulty, making it a little easier to think clearly and feel more grounded.
- One approach that many people find helpful is slow, diaphragmatic breathing — sometimes called "belly breathing." This involves breathing in gently through the nose for around four counts, allowing the belly to rise rather than the chest, then exhaling slowly through the mouth for a longer count of six or eight. The extended exhale is key, as it gently activates the body's natural calming response, helping to ease some of the physical sensations that come with anxiety.
- Another widely used technique is known as box breathing. This involves breathing in for four counts, holding for four, breathing out for four, and holding again for four — completing a steady, even cycle. This structured rhythm can be particularly useful during moments of acute stress, as it gives the mind something to focus on alongside the breath.
- A third option is the 5-4-3-2-1 grounding technique, which pairs breathing with sensory awareness — noticing five things you can see, four you can touch, and so on. Combining slow breathing with this kind of gentle sensory check-in can help bring attention back to the present moment when anxious thoughts feel distant from reality.
- These strategies can be practised anywhere and take only a few minutes. It is worth remembering that anxiety is a common human experience, and support is available for anyone who feels it is affecting their daily life. Speaking with a healthcare professional is a good first step toward finding the right kind of help.
