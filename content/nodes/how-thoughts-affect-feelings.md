- The connection between our thoughts and feelings is one of the most powerful — and most overlooked — aspects of our mental wellbeing. Most of us experience emotions as things that simply happen to us, arriving without warning or reason. But research suggests that the way we interpret events, rather than the events themselves, plays a significant role in shaping how we feel.
- This idea sits at the heart of cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), a widely studied approach to mental health. CBT is based on the understanding that our thoughts, feelings, and behaviours are closely linked. When we notice a situation and attach a meaning to it — however automatically — that meaning influences our emotional response. For example, if a friend doesn't reply to a message and we think "they must be angry with me," we are likely to feel anxious or upset. If instead we think "they're probably just busy," the emotional response tends to be much more neutral. The situation is identical; the interpretation makes the difference.
- One strategy many people find helpful is simply noticing thoughts, rather than immediately accepting them as facts. This is sometimes called "cognitive distancing" — creating a small pause between a thought and a reaction. Asking "is this thought definitely true, or is it one possible interpretation?" can gently open up space for a different perspective. Another approach involves keeping a brief thought journal — writing down recurring thoughts and the feelings they seem to produce. Over time, patterns often become visible, and patterns can be worked with.
- It is worth noting that unhelpful thinking styles are very common and are not a sign of weakness or failure. Many people find that, with time and the right support, they are able to develop a healthier relationship with their own thoughts. If you feel that your thoughts are significantly affecting your mood or daily life, speaking with a mental health professional can be a meaningful first step.
