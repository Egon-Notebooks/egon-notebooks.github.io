- Relationships shape so much of how we feel about ourselves and the world around us. Whether with a partner, family member, friend, or colleague, the patterns we fall into with others can quietly influence our mental health — sometimes in ways that are hard to notice from the inside.
- One of the most common signs of an unhealthy relationship pattern is a persistent feeling of walking on eggshells — a sense that you need to carefully manage your words or behaviour to avoid upsetting someone, or that conflict feels unpredictable or disproportionate. Many people also notice a gradual erosion of their confidence over time, finding that they second-guess themselves more than they used to, or feel a growing sense of shame or self-doubt that wasn't there before. These shifts can happen slowly, which is part of what makes them difficult to recognise.
- Another pattern worth being aware of is imbalance — where one person's needs, feelings, or opinions consistently take priority over the other's. Healthy relationships generally involve a sense of mutual respect and give-and-take, even if this looks different at various points in time. When one person regularly feels unheard, dismissed, or responsible for managing the emotional atmosphere, that imbalance can become a source of significant stress and low mood.
- It can also help to notice how you feel after spending time with someone. Many people find that checking in with themselves — asking honestly whether they tend to feel uplifted, neutral, or consistently drained — can offer useful information about whether a relationship is nourishing or depleting.
- Recognising these patterns is not about assigning blame, and it does not mean a relationship is beyond repair or that a situation cannot change. Becoming more aware is itself a meaningful step. Speaking with a trusted person or a mental health professional can help you make sense of what you are experiencing and explore what feels right for you.
