- Negative thinking patterns are something almost everyone experiences at some point. These are habitual ways of interpreting events that tend to be overly critical, pessimistic, or catastrophic — and they can quietly shape how we feel about ourselves, others, and the world around us. Recognising that these patterns are common, and that they can change, is an encouraging place to start.
- One of the most helpful things a person can do is simply begin to notice their thoughts, rather than automatically accepting them as facts. Cognitive behavioural approaches, which are well-supported by research, suggest that there is often a gap between what we think and what is actually true. Gently questioning a negative thought — asking "Is there another way to look at this?" or "What would I say to a friend who thought this way?" — can gradually loosen its hold. This takes practice, and it is normal for it to feel difficult at first.
- Another strategy many people find useful is grounding themselves in the present moment. Negative thinking often pulls us toward dwelling on the past or worrying about the future. Mindfulness practices, even simple ones like focusing on the breath for a few minutes, can help create a small but meaningful pause between a thought and our reaction to it. Over time, this can support a greater sense of calm and perspective.
- It also helps to pay attention to patterns over time. Keeping a brief journal of recurring thoughts can reveal themes that might otherwise go unnoticed, making it easier to address them in a more focused way.
- If negative thinking is persistent, intrusive, or significantly affecting daily life, speaking with a mental health professional can make a real difference. Support is available, and with the right help, many people find that their relationship with their own thoughts can shift in meaningful and lasting ways.
