- Burnout is a state of deep exhaustion — emotional, physical, and mental — that can develop after a prolonged period of stress, overwork, or feeling undervalued. It is more than simply feeling tired after a busy week. Many people describe it as a sense of emptiness, detachment, or a loss of motivation for things that once felt meaningful. If this sounds familiar, it is worth knowing that burnout is widely recognised, well understood, and something people do recover from.
- One of the most important steps in recovering from burnout is allowing yourself to slow down without guilt. Many people who experience burnout have spent a long time pushing through exhaustion rather than responding to it. Rest is not a reward for productivity — it is a genuine necessity, and giving yourself permission to rest is often the first step toward rebuilding energy and wellbeing.
- Reconnecting with small moments of meaning can also support recovery. Burnout often disconnects people from activities, relationships, and values that usually sustain them. Some people find it helpful to gently reintroduce one or two enjoyable or calming activities — not to fill a schedule, but simply to notice what still brings comfort or a small sense of pleasure. This might be time in nature, a conversation with someone trusted, or a creative outlet. There is no pressure to rediscover everything at once.
- Looking at boundaries and workload, whether with the help of a trusted person or a professional, can also make a meaningful difference over time. Understanding what contributed to burnout can help in making gradual changes that protect wellbeing going forward.
- Recovery from burnout is rarely linear, and it often takes longer than people expect — but it does happen. Reaching out for professional support is a sign of self-awareness, not weakness, and many people find that having the right support makes the process feel far more manageable.
