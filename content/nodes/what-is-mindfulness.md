- Mindfulness is a simple but powerful practice that involves paying attention to the present moment — what you are thinking, feeling, and sensing right now — with openness and without judgement. Rather than something mystical or complicated, mindfulness is a skill that anyone can develop, and research suggests it can play a meaningful role in supporting mental and emotional wellbeing.
- One of the core ideas behind mindfulness is noticing when the mind has wandered — to worries about the future or regrets about the past — and gently bringing attention back to the present. Many people find that even a few minutes of focused breathing each day can help create a small sense of calm and grounding. This might involve sitting quietly and simply observing each breath as it comes and goes, without trying to change or fix anything.
- Mindfulness can also be practised in everyday moments, not only during dedicated meditation time. Paying close attention to the taste of a meal, the sensation of walking, or the sounds in a room are all ways of bringing mindful awareness into ordinary life. Some people find that this kind of gentle noticing helps them feel less caught up in anxious thoughts or overwhelming emotions, and more able to respond to difficulties with a little more steadiness.
- It is worth knowing that mindfulness is not about clearing the mind completely or achieving a particular feeling — some sessions may feel restful, others less so, and both are entirely normal. Like any skill, it tends to develop gradually with practice.
- If you are curious about mindfulness as part of looking after your mental health, you do not have to figure it out alone. Talking to a healthcare professional or a qualified therapist can help you explore whether mindfulness-based approaches might be a good fit for you, alongside any other support you may need.
