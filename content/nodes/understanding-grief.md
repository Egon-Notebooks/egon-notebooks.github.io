- Grief is one of the most universal human experiences — and one of the most deeply personal. Whether it follows the loss of a loved one, the end of a relationship, a change in health, or any other significant loss, grief can affect every part of life: thoughts, emotions, physical sensations, and daily routines. There is no single "right" way to grieve, and understanding this can itself be a source of comfort.
- One thing many people find helpful is giving themselves permission to feel whatever arises. Grief rarely moves in a straight line. Some days may feel manageable; others may bring unexpected waves of sadness, anger, numbness, or even moments of relief or laughter. All of these responses are normal. Resisting or judging these feelings can sometimes make them harder to process, while allowing them space — perhaps through journalling, talking to someone trusted, or simply sitting with them — may support the natural process of adjustment over time.
- Connection can also play an important role in navigating grief. Many people find that sharing memories of what or who was lost, or simply being in the company of others who care about them, offers a sense of grounding. It can be equally valid to need quiet and solitude at times. Listening to what feels right on any given day, rather than following external expectations about how grief "should" look, is something many people find meaningful.
- It is also worth knowing that grief does not have a set timeline, and finding life difficult for a sustained period is not a sign of weakness. Many people discover that support from a trained professional — such as a counsellor or therapist — can offer a safe, compassionate space to process loss in a way that feels manageable. Reaching out is a sign of courage, and support is available.
