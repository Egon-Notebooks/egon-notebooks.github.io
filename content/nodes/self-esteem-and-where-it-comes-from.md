- Self-esteem is the way we see and value ourselves — the quiet inner voice that shapes how worthy, capable, or loveable we feel day to day. It influences how we approach challenges, relate to others, and respond to setbacks. Understanding where self-esteem comes from can be a meaningful first step toward nurturing a healthier relationship with ourselves.
- Self-esteem begins forming early in life, shaped by our experiences, relationships, and the messages we receive from the world around us. Encouraging words from caregivers, teachers, or peers can build a sense of inner worth, while repeated criticism or difficult experiences may leave a person feeling less confident or deserving. Importantly, these early influences are not the whole story — self-esteem is not fixed, and it continues to evolve throughout our lives.
- One insight that many people find helpful is recognising that self-esteem is not the same as achievement or approval. When our sense of worth depends entirely on external validation — praise, success, or others' opinions — it can feel fragile and inconsistent. Many people find that practising self-compassion, the ability to treat oneself with the same kindness offered to a good friend, can gently shift this foundation toward something more stable and internal.
- Another approach that may help is noticing and gradually challenging the inner critic. Negative self-talk often feels like truth, but these thoughts are patterns that can be explored and, over time, questioned. Small, consistent actions — setting achievable goals, acknowledging personal strengths, or spending time with people who are supportive — can contribute to a more balanced sense of self-worth.
- It is entirely normal for self-esteem to fluctuate, and many people experience periods where it feels low. This does not reflect a permanent truth about who someone is. Support is available, and with the right tools and guidance, developing a kinder, more compassionate view of oneself is genuinely possible.
