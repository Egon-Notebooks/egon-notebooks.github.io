- Depression can make even the smallest tasks feel exhausting. When your energy reserves are low, everyday life — getting dressed, preparing food, responding to a message — can feel like climbing a steep hill. This is a recognised part of how depression affects the body and mind, and it is something many people navigate with the right support and strategies.
- One approach that many people find helpful is the idea of working with your energy rather than against it. Instead of pushing through a long list of tasks, try identifying just one or two small, meaningful things to focus on during periods when you feel slightly more alert — even if that window is brief. Letting go of the pressure to be productive across an entire day can reduce the added burden of self-criticism, which often drains energy further.
- Rest, too, deserves to be treated as an active part of managing depression, not a sign of failure. Many people find that building in deliberate, guilt-free rest — rather than collapsing from exhaustion — helps them feel more in control. Equally, gentle movement, such as a short walk or light stretching, can sometimes support energy levels over time, even when it feels difficult to begin. Starting very small is entirely valid.
- It can also help to simplify choices where possible. Decision-making takes real mental effort, and reducing the number of small decisions in a day — by preparing easy meals, laying out clothes the night before, or following a loose routine — can free up a little more capacity for what matters most to you.
- Living with low energy due to depression can feel isolating, but it is something many people experience and work through. Speaking with a healthcare professional or counsellor can open up further options for support, and small steps, taken at your own pace, can genuinely make a difference over time.
