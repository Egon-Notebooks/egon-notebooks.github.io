- Perfectionism is something many people relate to — that inner drive to get things just right, to meet high standards, or to avoid making mistakes. While having goals and caring about quality can be genuinely valuable, perfectionism becomes a concern when the pursuit of flawlessness starts to cause stress, self-criticism, or avoidance. Understanding the difference can be an important first step toward a healthier relationship with yourself and your expectations.
- One thing many people find helpful is noticing the difference between striving for excellence and demanding perfection. Excellence allows for effort, learning, and occasional setbacks. Perfectionism, by contrast, often involves harsh self-judgement when anything falls short of an impossibly high bar. Gently challenging that inner critic — asking whether you would speak to a friend the way you speak to yourself — can begin to shift that pattern over time.
- Another strategy that some people find useful is practising what psychologists call "adaptive imperfection" — intentionally doing something in a good-enough way and observing that the feared consequences often do not materialise. This might mean sending an email without re-reading it five times, or finishing a creative project rather than endlessly refining it. Small, repeated experiences like these can gradually build tolerance for uncertainty and help loosen perfectionism's grip.
- It can also be worth exploring where perfectionist thinking comes from. For many people, it is connected to deeper feelings about self-worth — a sense that love, acceptance, or value must be earned through achievement. Recognising this connection, with compassion rather than judgement, can be a meaningful part of the process.
- If perfectionism is affecting your wellbeing, your relationships, or your ability to function day to day, you do not have to navigate it alone. Support is available, and many people find that working with a professional helps them develop a kinder, more sustainable relationship with themselves.
