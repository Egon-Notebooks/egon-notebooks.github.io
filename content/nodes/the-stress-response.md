- The body's stress response is a natural, built-in system designed to help us cope with challenges. When we sense a threat — whether it's a looming deadline, a difficult conversation, or an unexpected crisis — the brain triggers a cascade of physical and emotional changes. Understanding how this response works can help us relate to it with more curiosity and less fear.
- When stress is activated, the body releases hormones such as adrenaline and cortisol, which raise the heart rate, sharpen focus, and prepare muscles for action. This is sometimes called the "fight-or-flight" response, and in short bursts it can be genuinely useful — helping us meet a challenge or stay alert in a demanding situation. Many people find that recognising these physical sensations as a normal bodily process, rather than a sign that something is wrong, can make them feel more manageable in the moment.
- The difficulty arises when stress becomes prolonged or overwhelming. Chronic stress — where the body stays in a heightened state over a long period — can affect sleep, concentration, mood, and physical health. Some strategies that may help include building regular periods of rest into the day, engaging in gentle physical movement, practising slow and deliberate breathing, and talking to someone trusted about what feels difficult. Research suggests that even small, consistent habits can gradually support the nervous system in returning to a calmer baseline.
- It is also worth remembering that stress affects people differently, and what feels manageable for one person may feel intense for another. There is no single "right" way to respond. If stress feels persistent, unmanageable, or is getting in the way of daily life, speaking with a health professional is a meaningful and worthwhile step. Support is available, and with the right help, things can shift.
