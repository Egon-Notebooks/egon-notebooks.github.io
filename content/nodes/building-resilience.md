- Resilience is often described as the ability to adapt and recover in the face of adversity, stress, or difficult life events. It is not a fixed trait that some people are born with and others are not — resilience is something that can be nurtured and strengthened over time, at any stage of life.
- One of the most consistent findings in resilience research is the importance of connection. Maintaining relationships with people we trust — whether friends, family, or community — can provide a sense of belonging and a buffer during hard times. These connections do not need to be numerous; even a small number of meaningful relationships can make a meaningful difference to how supported a person feels when facing challenges.
- Another strategy that many people find helpful is developing a sense of self-awareness around how they respond to stress. This might involve noticing unhelpful thought patterns and gently questioning them, or finding small, grounding routines that provide stability during uncertain periods. Practices such as regular physical activity, spending time in nature, or engaging in creative outlets are examples of activities that some people find help regulate their mood and build a sense of inner steadiness over time.
- It can also be valuable to reflect on past difficulties that have already been navigated. Looking back at challenges that once felt overwhelming, and recognising the strengths and resources that helped in getting through them, can offer a reminder that difficulty does not last forever and that personal capacity for growth is real.
- Resilience does not mean managing everything alone or never struggling. Asking for help is itself an act of strength. If life is feeling particularly hard to navigate, speaking with a mental health professional can offer guidance and support that is tailored to your individual experience. Nobody has to face difficult times without support.
