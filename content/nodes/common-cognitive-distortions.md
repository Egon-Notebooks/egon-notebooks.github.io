- Thoughts are powerful — but they are not always accurate. Many people, at various points in their lives, notice patterns of thinking that feel convincing in the moment yet don't quite reflect reality. These patterns are sometimes called cognitive distortions, which simply means habitual ways of thinking that can be unhelpful or overly negative. Recognising them is often an important first step toward feeling better.
- One common pattern is "all-or-nothing thinking," where situations feel entirely good or entirely bad, with no middle ground. A person might believe that one mistake makes them a complete failure, or that if something isn't perfect it has no value at all. Another frequent pattern is "catastrophising," which involves assuming the worst possible outcome is likely or inevitable — for example, making one error at work and becoming convinced it will lead to serious consequences. There is also "mind reading," where someone assumes they know what others are thinking, usually imagining it to be something critical or negative, without any real evidence.
- Some strategies that many people find helpful include gently questioning the evidence behind a thought — asking "what are the facts here?" or "is there another way to look at this situation?" Keeping a brief journal of recurring thoughts can also help people notice patterns over time. Practising self-compassion — treating yourself with the same kindness you might offer a friend — is another approach that research consistently supports.
- It is worth knowing that these thought patterns are very common and are not a sign of weakness or permanent limitation. With time, support, and the right tools, many people find that their relationship with their own thinking shifts in meaningful ways. If unhelpful thoughts are affecting your daily life, speaking with a mental health professional can make a real difference — support is available, and things can change.
