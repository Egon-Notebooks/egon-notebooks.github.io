- Certain dates can carry unexpected emotional weight. An anniversary reaction is a shift in mood, thoughts, or behaviour that occurs around the time of a significant past event — such as a loss, a trauma, or a major life change. Many people notice feeling low, anxious, irritable, or unsettled without immediately connecting those feelings to the date. This is a well-recognised and very human response, and understanding it can make it feel a little less overwhelming.
- One of the most helpful things a person can do is simply notice the pattern. If difficult feelings tend to surface around a particular time of year, reflecting on whether those dates hold significance can bring a sense of clarity. Naming what is happening — "this is an anniversary reaction" — does not take the feelings away, but it can help a person feel less confused or alarmed by them. Journalling, talking with a trusted friend, or spending time in a way that feels meaningful are approaches that many people find grounding during these periods.
- It can also help to plan ahead. Some people find it useful to be a little gentler with themselves in the days surrounding a significant date — adjusting expectations, reducing unnecessary pressures, and making space for whatever comes up emotionally. Marking the occasion in a personal, intentional way, such as through a small ritual or act of remembrance, can offer a sense of meaning and acknowledgement rather than avoidance.
- Anniversary reactions are a normal part of how we carry our experiences through time. They do not mean something has gone wrong in a person's healing, nor that they are stuck. If these reactions feel intense, persistent, or difficult to manage alone, speaking with a mental health professional can offer real support. Reaching out is a sign of self-awareness, and help is available.
