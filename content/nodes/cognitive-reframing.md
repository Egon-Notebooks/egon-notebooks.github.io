- Cognitive reframing is a skill rooted in the idea that the way we interpret events can shape how we feel about them. While we often cannot control what happens to us, we do have some capacity to influence how we make sense of our experiences. Learning to gently examine and shift unhelpful thought patterns is something many people find supportive as part of caring for their mental wellbeing.
- One approach is to notice when a thought feels absolute or extreme — phrases like "I always fail" or "nothing ever works out" can be a signal to pause. Many people find it helpful to ask themselves whether there is another way to view the situation, or what they might say to a close friend who had the same thought. This isn't about forcing positivity or dismissing genuine difficulties — it's about creating a little space between a thought and the weight we assign to it.
- Another useful practice involves examining the evidence. When a difficult thought arises, some people find it grounding to ask: what facts support this thought, and what facts might challenge it? This isn't about talking yourself out of your feelings, but about checking whether a thought is a reflection of reality or a reflection of how hard things feel in that moment. The two can look very similar, especially during stressful or painful periods.
- Cognitive reframing also takes practice — it is a skill that tends to develop gradually rather than something that shifts all at once. Many people find it most effective when explored with the support of a trained therapist, particularly those working with approaches like cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), which is a structured, evidence-based talking therapy.
- If you have been finding it difficult to manage difficult thoughts on your own, reaching out for support is a meaningful and courageous step. Help is available, and things can change.
