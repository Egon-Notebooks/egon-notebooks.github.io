- Living in a way that feels meaningful can have a powerful effect on overall wellbeing. One of the most grounding things a person can do is take time to explore their values — the principles and qualities that matter most to them. When daily life feels disconnected from what we genuinely care about, it can contribute to feelings of emptiness, stress, or low mood. Understanding your values can offer a quiet but steady compass.
- Values are deeply personal. They might include things like honesty, creativity, connection, independence, or kindness — there is no right or wrong answer. A useful starting point is to reflect on moments when you felt most like yourself, or times when something felt unexpectedly important to you. Many people find it helpful to ask: "When have I felt proud, or fulfilled, or at peace?" The themes that appear in those memories often point toward what matters most.
- It can also be worthwhile to notice where tension or frustration shows up in daily life. Sometimes a persistent sense of dissatisfaction signals that a value is being overlooked or compromised — perhaps a need for creativity in a very rigid routine, or a longing for deeper connection. Recognising this is not a reason for self-criticism, but an invitation to gently explore what small adjustments might bring life into closer alignment with what feels true. Some people find that writing freely about these reflections, or talking them through with someone trusted, helps to bring greater clarity.
- Identifying your values is not a one-time exercise — they can shift and deepen across different stages of life. If you are finding it difficult to connect with what matters to you, or if a sense of purposelessness is weighing heavily, speaking with a mental health professional can be a meaningful and helpful step. Support is available, and things can change.
