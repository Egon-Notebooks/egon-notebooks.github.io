- It can be easy, especially during difficult periods, to focus on what feels broken or lacking within ourselves. Yet each person carries a unique set of strengths — qualities, skills, and ways of being that have helped them navigate life, even when times have been hard. Learning to recognise those strengths is not about ignoring real challenges; it is about building a fuller, more balanced picture of who you are.
- One place to start is by reflecting on moments when you have coped, adapted, or supported someone else. Many people find it helpful to think about a difficult experience they have moved through — not necessarily with ease, but with some degree of persistence. What did that take? Qualities like patience, courage, humour, or loyalty often emerge in hindsight, qualities that were present all along but easy to overlook. Keeping a simple journal and noting one thing you handled or appreciated about yourself each day can gradually shift the way you see yourself over time.
- It can also be valuable to consider the perspective of people who know you well. Trusted friends, family members, or colleagues often notice strengths that we are too close to see in ourselves. Asking someone you trust what they feel you bring to their life, or to a situation, can be a gentle and grounding exercise. Their answers may surprise you.
- Recognising your strengths is not a fixed destination — it is something that can deepen and shift as you grow. It is also closely connected to wellbeing; research consistently suggests that people who are aware of their personal strengths tend to experience greater resilience and a stronger sense of purpose. If you find it hard to identify anything positive in yourself, that in itself can be worth exploring with a professional. Support is available, and you do not have to work through this alone.
