- Panic attacks can be an overwhelming and frightening experience. The sudden rush of intense fear, accompanied by physical sensations like a racing heart, shortness of breath, or dizziness, can feel completely out of nowhere — and for many people, deeply confusing. It is important to know that panic attacks, while deeply uncomfortable, are not dangerous, and they do pass. You are not alone in experiencing them.
- Understanding what is happening in the body during a panic attack can itself be reassuring. The brain's threat-response system becomes activated, flooding the body with adrenaline as though it were facing danger. This is an entirely natural physiological process — it just happens at a time when there is no actual threat present. Recognising this does not make the experience any less real, but many people find that this knowledge helps reduce some of the fear around the sensations themselves.
- There are some strategies that may help both during and between panic attacks. Slow, controlled breathing — such as breathing in for four counts, holding briefly, and breathing out for six counts — can help gently signal to the nervous system that it is safe to calm down. Grounding techniques, like noticing five things you can see or focusing on the feeling of your feet on the floor, may also help to bring attention back to the present moment. Over time, working to understand the patterns and triggers of panic, perhaps through approaches like cognitive behavioural therapy, has helped many people experience panic attacks less frequently and feel less afraid of them when they do occur.
- Living with panic can feel isolating, but it is a very common human experience, and things can genuinely improve. Reaching out to a GP or mental health professional is a positive and courageous step — support is available, and with the right help, many people find that panic becomes far more manageable.
