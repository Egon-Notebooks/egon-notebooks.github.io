- Self-reflection is the practice of turning your attention inward — noticing your thoughts, feelings, and experiences with curiosity rather than judgement. It is a skill that many people find helpful for understanding themselves more clearly, navigating difficult emotions, and building a greater sense of wellbeing over time. Like any skill, it can be developed gradually, and there is no single "right" way to do it.
- One approach that many people find accessible is journalling. Setting aside even a few minutes each day to write freely about how you are feeling — without editing or censoring yourself — can help bring clarity to thoughts that might otherwise feel tangled or overwhelming. You do not need to write in full sentences or follow any particular format. The goal is simply to create a quiet space for honest self-expression.
- Mindful pausing is another practice worth exploring. This involves briefly checking in with yourself throughout the day — noticing what emotions are present, how your body feels, and what might be influencing your mood. Many people find that even a short, intentional pause can help them respond to challenging situations more thoughtfully, rather than reacting on autopilot.
- Reflective questioning can also be a gentle way to build self-awareness. Questions such as "What do I need right now?" or "What is this feeling trying to tell me?" invite a compassionate, exploratory approach to your inner world. Over time, this kind of curiosity can help you recognise patterns and understand your emotional needs more clearly.
- Self-reflection is not about finding fault or dwelling on the past — it is about getting to know yourself with kindness. If you find that exploring your inner world brings up difficult or distressing feelings, reaching out to a trusted mental health professional can be a meaningful and courageous step. Support is available, and things can change.
