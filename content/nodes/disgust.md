- Disgust is one of our most powerful emotions — and one of the least talked about in mental health conversations. While it evolved to protect us from harm, disgust can also turn inward, shaping how people feel about themselves, their bodies, their past experiences, or their thoughts. When this happens, it can quietly undermine wellbeing in ways that are easy to overlook but important to understand.
- One area where self-directed disgust often appears is in the way people relate to their own bodies. Many people find that practising small moments of body neutrality — focusing on what the body does rather than how it looks — can gently soften this response over time. This is not about forcing positive feelings, but about gradually loosening the grip of harsh self-judgement.
- Disgust can also arise in response to intrusive thoughts, memories, or past behaviours. When this happens, people sometimes withdraw from support or feel too ashamed to speak openly about what they are experiencing. Some find it helpful to approach these feelings with curiosity rather than avoidance — noticing the disgust without acting on it or believing it defines them. Therapeutic approaches such as compassion-focused therapy and acceptance and commitment therapy have been developed specifically to help people work with shame and self-directed disgust in a supported, structured way.
- It is also worth knowing that disgust experienced in relation to trauma is a recognised and understandable response. Feeling this way does not reflect a person's worth or character. With the right support, many people find that the intensity of these feelings can shift, and a more compassionate relationship with oneself becomes possible.
- If disgust — whether directed at yourself or the world — feels persistent or overwhelming, speaking with a mental health professional can be a meaningful and helpful step. You do not have to navigate these feelings alone.
