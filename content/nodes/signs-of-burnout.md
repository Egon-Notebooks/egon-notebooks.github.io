- Burnout is a state of deep exhaustion that can develop gradually when stress — particularly from work or caregiving — goes unaddressed for a long time. It is not a sign of weakness or failure, but a very human response to sustained pressure without enough rest or recovery. Recognising the signs early can make a real difference in how a person responds and gets support.
- One of the most common signs of burnout is a persistent sense of emotional and physical exhaustion that does not improve with rest. A person experiencing burnout may wake up feeling just as tired as when they went to sleep, and find that activities they once enjoyed feel flat or draining. This goes beyond ordinary tiredness — it can feel as though the energy needed to engage with daily life has simply run out.
- Burnout can also affect how a person thinks and feels about their responsibilities. Many people notice a growing sense of cynicism, detachment, or feeling like their efforts no longer matter. Concentration and decision-making can become harder, and small tasks that were once manageable may start to feel overwhelming. Alongside this, some people experience physical symptoms such as frequent headaches, changes in sleep patterns, or a lowered immune system.
- A third sign worth paying attention to is a quiet withdrawal from people and activities. Someone experiencing burnout may find themselves pulling back from relationships, losing their sense of purpose, or feeling a persistent low mood that is difficult to shake. These feelings are meaningful signals worth taking seriously.
- If any of this feels familiar, it is worth knowing that burnout is recognised and understood, and that many people have moved through it with the right support. Speaking with a trusted person or a mental health professional can be a helpful first step — things do not have to stay this way, and support is available.
