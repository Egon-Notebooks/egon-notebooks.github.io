- Grief is one of the most universal human experiences, yet it can feel profoundly isolating — both for the person living through it and for those who care about them. When someone we love is grieving, it is natural to want to take their pain away. Knowing that we cannot can leave us feeling helpless or unsure of what to say. The truth is, being a supportive presence does not require having the right words. It requires showing up with patience, openness, and care.
- One of the most meaningful things you can offer is simply your presence. Many people find that sitting with someone in silence, checking in with a brief message, or helping with a practical task — like cooking a meal or running an errand — can feel more comforting than any carefully chosen phrase. Grief does not follow a set timeline, and some of the most valuable support comes weeks or months later, when others have moved on but the loss still feels very close.
- It also helps to follow the grieving person's lead. Some people need to talk about the person they have lost; others need distraction or lightness. Asking gently, "Would you like to talk, or would you prefer some company without conversation?" gives them agency over their own experience. Try to avoid phrases that minimise the loss, such as "everything happens for a reason" or "at least they are no longer suffering" — even when well-intentioned, these can feel dismissive. Instead, simply acknowledging the pain — "I am so sorry, this is really hard" — is often enough.
- Supporting a grieving person can also be emotionally demanding, and it is important to tend to your own wellbeing too. If you or someone you care about is finding grief particularly difficult to carry, professional support is available and can make a real difference. Reaching out is a sign of strength, not weakness.
