# Rumination and how to interrupt it

Rumination — the tendency to replay worries, regrets, or upsetting events over and over in the mind — is something many people experience at some point in their lives. While it can feel like problem-solving, research suggests that getting stuck in repetitive thought loops rarely leads to solutions. Instead, it can deepen feelings of sadness, anxiety, or stress. The good news is that rumination is not a fixed habit, and there are gentle, practical ways to interrupt it.

One strategy many people find helpful is engaging the body to shift attention away from the mind. Physical movement — even a short walk — can help break the cycle by redirecting focus outward. Activities that require concentration, such as cooking, drawing, or a puzzle, can also give the mind something concrete to engage with, creating a natural pause in repetitive thinking.

Another approach is to practise noticing without judgment. When rumination begins, simply observing the thought — "I'm going over this again" — without trying to force it away can reduce its grip. This kind of mindful awareness, even in small doses, helps create a little distance between a person and the thoughts that feel so consuming. Over time, this distance can make it easier to choose where attention goes.

It can also help to set a specific, limited time to think through a worry — sometimes called "scheduled worry time" — and then gently redirect attention when thoughts arise outside that window. This can give the mind a sense of permission to process, without letting rumination run unchecked throughout the day.

If rumination is frequent or feels overwhelming, it is worth knowing that support is available. Talking with a mental health professional can help someone understand the patterns behind their thinking and develop tools that work for them. Many people find that, with the right support, these cycles can become much easier to manage.
