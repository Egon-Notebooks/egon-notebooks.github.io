# Ambiguous loss

Grief does not always come with a clear ending. Sometimes loss occurs without a death, a goodbye, or a defined moment of change — and this kind of loss can be among the hardest to make sense of. This experience is known as ambiguous loss, a term used to describe grief that arises when someone or something is gone, but the loss feels incomplete or unresolved. It might involve a loved one who is physically present but psychologically absent, such as someone living with dementia or a severe mental health condition. It can also describe the opposite — someone who is emotionally significant but physically gone, such as an estranged family member, a missing person, or a child lost to adoption. In both cases, there is no clear closure, and the grief can feel confusing, even invisible.

One of the most important things to understand about ambiguous loss is that the absence of a clear ending does not mean the grief is any less real. Many people find it helpful to name what they are experiencing — simply recognizing "this is a real loss" can ease some of the isolation and self-doubt that often accompanies it. Allowing space to feel sadness, anger, or confusion, without expecting a tidy resolution, can be a meaningful step.

Finding ways to hold both the loss and the continuing relationship — in whatever form it takes — is something many people find useful over time. This might look like creating new rituals, reconnecting with community, or exploring meaning through reflection, creativity, or conversation with trusted people.

Ambiguous loss can be a long and nonlinear journey, and it is one that no one needs to navigate alone. Talking with a mental health professional who understands grief can provide meaningful support, and many people find that, with time and care, it becomes possible to carry loss with greater ease.
