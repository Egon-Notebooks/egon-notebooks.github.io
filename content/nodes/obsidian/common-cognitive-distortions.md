# Common cognitive distortions

Thoughts can feel like facts — but they often aren't. Cognitive distortions are patterns of thinking that can feel completely convincing in the moment, yet tend to be inaccurate or unhelpful. They are extremely common, and recognizing them is a meaningful first step toward thinking in ways that feel less overwhelming.

One of the most widespread patterns is "all-or-nothing thinking," where situations are seen in absolute terms — something is either a total success or a complete failure, with no middle ground. Another common pattern is "catastrophizing," which involves assuming the worst possible outcome is likely, even when the evidence doesn't support it. A third is "mind reading," where a person feels certain they know what others are thinking — usually something negative — without any real basis for that belief. These patterns are not character flaws or signs of weakness. They tend to develop as mental shortcuts, and many people experience them at some point in their lives.

Some strategies that may help include learning to pause and notice a thought before automatically accepting it as true. Many people find it useful to ask themselves what evidence supports or contradicts a particular thought, or how they might respond if a friend shared the same worry with them. Writing thoughts down can also help create a little distance between the feeling and the belief, making it easier to examine them more gently and clearly.

Becoming aware of cognitive distortions doesn't mean forcing yourself to "think positively." It simply means opening up more space between a thought and how you respond to it. This kind of work takes practice, and support from a trained mental health professional — such as through cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) — can make the process significantly more manageable. Reaching out for that support is a genuinely hopeful step.
