# Shame and guilt

Shame and guilt are emotions that almost everyone encounters at some point, yet they are often misunderstood — and the difference between them matters more than many people realise. While both can feel heavy and uncomfortable, understanding what each emotion is communicating can be an important step toward processing them in a healthy way.

Guilt tends to focus on a specific behaviour — a sense that something you did was wrong or hurtful. It can be a useful signal, prompting reflection, repair, and change. Shame, on the other hand, turns inward. Rather than "I did something bad," shame carries the message "I am bad." This distinction is significant, because while guilt can motivate positive action, shame often leads people to hide, withdraw, or feel fundamentally flawed in a way that can be difficult to shift.

One strategy many people find helpful is learning to name the emotion clearly. Simply recognising "this is shame" or "this is guilt" can create a small but meaningful sense of distance, making the feeling easier to examine rather than be consumed by. Another approach involves practising self-compassion — treating yourself with the same kindness you might offer a friend who was struggling. Research consistently suggests that self-compassion does not make people complacent; in fact, it tends to support healthier motivation and accountability. Talking about these feelings, whether with a trusted person in your life or a professional, can also reduce their intensity. Shame in particular tends to grow in silence and lose some of its power when brought into conversation.

If shame or guilt has been weighing on you for some time, or feels difficult to manage alone, reaching out for support is a meaningful and courageous step. These emotions are common, they are not permanent, and with the right support, many people find that they can begin to move forward.
