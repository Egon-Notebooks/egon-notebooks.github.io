# Surprise and awe

Surprise and awe are among the most powerful emotional experiences available to us — those moments when something stops us in our tracks and reminds us that the world is larger, stranger, and more beautiful than our everyday routines suggest. Whether it arrives through a stunning landscape, an unexpected act of kindness, or a piece of music that seems to reach somewhere deep inside, awe has a quiet but meaningful relationship with our mental wellbeing.

Research in psychology suggests that experiences of awe can help shift our attention away from our own worries and preoccupations. When we encounter something that feels vast or hard to fully comprehend, our sense of self — including the inner critic and the endless to-do list — tends to quiet down for a moment. Many people find that this natural pause can bring a sense of calm and perspective that is difficult to achieve through deliberate effort alone.

Awe also appears to support a sense of connection. It often reminds us that we are part of something bigger than ourselves, whether that is a community, the natural world, or simply the shared experience of being human. Some people find that regularly seeking out small moments of wonder — noticing the detail in an ordinary leaf, watching the way light changes across a room, or listening closely to a story that moves them — can gently build a habit of openness and presence over time.

It is worth noting that awe does not require dramatic or extraordinary circumstances. Surprise and wonder can be found in quiet, everyday places, and cultivating a gentle curiosity about the world around you is something anyone can practice in their own way and at their own pace.

If you are finding it difficult to feel connected to the world around you, or if low mood is making it hard to notice moments of beauty, support is available and things can change.
