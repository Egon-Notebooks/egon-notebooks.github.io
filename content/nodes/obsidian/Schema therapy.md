# Schema therapy

Schema therapy is a type of psychotherapy that helps people identify and shift deep-rooted patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving — patterns that often develop early in life and continue to affect us well into adulthood. For many people, these patterns, known as "schemas," can quietly shape how they relate to themselves and others, sometimes in ways that feel difficult to understand or change. The good news is that with the right support, these patterns can be recognized, worked through, and transformed.

One of the core ideas in schema therapy is that many of our emotional struggles are connected to unmet needs from childhood — things like safety, connection, or validation. When those needs weren't fully met, the mind can develop protective responses that made sense at the time but may no longer serve us well. In therapy, a trained professional helps a person gently explore these early experiences without judgment, building greater self-awareness over time.

Schema therapy also draws on a range of techniques to help people move forward. These may include talking through past experiences, guided imagery exercises, and exploring how different emotional "modes" or states show up in daily life. Many people find that understanding why they feel or react the way they do — rather than simply trying to stop those reactions — brings a real sense of relief and opens the door to meaningful change.

Another aspect that many people appreciate about schema therapy is its emphasis on the therapeutic relationship itself. Feeling genuinely heard and supported by a therapist can be a powerful part of the healing process, particularly for those who have experienced difficult relationships in the past.

If you recognize patterns in your own life that feel stuck or hard to shift, speaking with a mental health professional can be a helpful first step. Support is available, and change is possible.
