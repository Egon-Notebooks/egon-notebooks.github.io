# Energy conservation when depressed

Feeling exhausted when you are experiencing depression is not a sign of weakness or laziness — it is one of the most common and challenging aspects of the condition. Depression can make even small, everyday tasks feel overwhelming, and the gap between what you want to do and what you feel capable of doing can itself become a source of distress. Understanding how to work with your energy, rather than against it, can make a meaningful difference on difficult days.

One approach many people find helpful is breaking tasks down into smaller steps. Rather than thinking about "cleaning the kitchen," for example, focusing on just washing a single cup can feel more achievable. Completing even a very small action can provide a gentle sense of accomplishment, which some research suggests may help ease the weight of low mood over time. The goal is not to do everything — it is to do something, however small.

Pacing is another strategy worth considering. This means spreading activities across the day or week in a way that avoids pushing through exhaustion and then crashing. Many people find it useful to identify the time of day when they feel slightly more able, even if that window is short, and to save meaningful or necessary tasks for those moments. Rest, too, is a legitimate and important part of this process — not a failure to cope.

It can also help to let go of comparisons with how much energy you had before, or how much others seem to manage. Depression affects the brain and body in real, physical ways, and your capacity right now may genuinely be different from usual. This is temporary, not permanent.

If low energy and low mood have been affecting your daily life, speaking with a healthcare professional is a worthwhile step. Support is available, and things can improve.
