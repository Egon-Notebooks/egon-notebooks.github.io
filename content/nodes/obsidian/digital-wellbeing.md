# Digital wellbeing

Screens are woven into almost every part of modern life — from work and socialising to rest and entertainment. While technology offers genuine connection and convenience, many people find that their relationship with digital devices can sometimes leave them feeling drained, distracted, or disconnected from the world around them. Paying attention to how technology affects your mood and energy is a meaningful step toward feeling more balanced.

One strategy many people find helpful is creating gentle boundaries around device use — not by cutting out technology entirely, but by introducing small moments of intentional distance. This might look like keeping the first or last thirty minutes of the day screen-free, or choosing to eat meals without scrolling. These pauses can offer the nervous system a chance to settle and allow space for other activities that feel nourishing.

It can also be worth reflecting on which types of digital activity tend to leave you feeling better or worse. Passive scrolling through social media, for example, affects people differently than a video call with a friend or an absorbing creative project online. Noticing these patterns — perhaps by keeping a brief journal or simply checking in with yourself after time online — can help you make more conscious choices about how you engage with technology.

Physical cues in your environment can support this too. Many people find that charging phones outside the bedroom, turning off non-essential notifications, or designating certain spaces as screen-free helps reduce the background hum of digital demands and creates room for rest and presence.

Finding a healthier rhythm with technology is something that looks different for everyone, and it can take time to figure out what works. If you notice that your digital habits are significantly affecting your sleep, mood, or relationships, speaking with a mental health professional can offer valuable support and perspective.
