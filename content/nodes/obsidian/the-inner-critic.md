# The inner critic

Many people are familiar with that persistent internal voice that points out flaws, replays mistakes, and quietly whispers that they are not quite good enough. This voice is often called the inner critic, and while it can feel overwhelming at times, understanding it is a meaningful first step toward building a kinder relationship with yourself.

The inner critic tends to develop early in life, often shaped by experiences, feedback from others, and the environments we grew up in. Recognizing that this voice is not an objective truth — but rather a pattern of thought that can be questioned and changed — can be genuinely freeing. Many people find it helpful to notice when their inner critic is speaking and to pause before accepting what it says as fact. Simply labeling the experience, such as thinking "that's my inner critic," can create a small but important sense of distance from the thought.

Practising self-compassion is another approach that research suggests can make a real difference. This means treating yourself with the same understanding and patience you might offer a close friend who is struggling. When a harsh self-critical thought arises, some people find it helpful to gently ask: "Would I say this to someone I care about?" Over time, this kind of reframing can soften the inner critic's grip and open space for a more balanced inner voice.

Mindfulness practices — bringing gentle, non-judgmental attention to thoughts as they arise — may also support people in observing self-critical patterns without becoming caught up in them. Rather than pushing difficult thoughts away or accepting them completely, mindfulness encourages a middle path of calm awareness.

If your inner critic feels particularly loud or is affecting your daily life, you do not have to navigate that alone. Support is available, and many people find that working with a professional helps them develop lasting tools for a more compassionate relationship with themselves.
