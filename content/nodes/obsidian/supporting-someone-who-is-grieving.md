# Supporting someone who is grieving

Grief is one of the most profound human experiences, and watching someone you care about move through loss can feel both deeply affecting and uncertain. Many people worry about saying the wrong thing or not knowing how to help — but simply showing up, in whatever way feels genuine, can matter more than finding the perfect words.

One of the most meaningful things you can offer is presence without pressure. This might look like sitting with someone in silence, sending a message to let them know you are thinking of them, or asking what kind of support they need rather than assuming. Grief does not follow a predictable path, and the person you are supporting may feel differently from one day to the next. Following their lead — rather than guiding them toward a particular way of grieving — can help them feel respected and understood.

Practical support is also something many people find genuinely helpful when they are grieving. Everyday tasks can feel overwhelming during periods of loss, so offering something specific — such as cooking a meal, helping with errands, or looking after children or pets for an afternoon — can ease some of that weight. Vague offers like "let me know if you need anything" are well-intentioned but can be hard to act on when someone is struggling, so a concrete suggestion is often more useful.

It is also worth remembering to continue checking in over time. The early days of grief often bring a lot of attention and support, but the weeks and months that follow can feel isolating. A simple message or visit, long after the initial loss, can remind someone that they are not forgotten.

Supporting a grieving person can be emotionally demanding, and it is okay to seek guidance or support for yourself too. Grief counsellors and mental health professionals can offer both the bereaved and their loved ones a space to process and find a way forward.
