# Body image and self-acceptance

Feeling comfortable in your own skin is something many people find genuinely challenging. Body image — the way we perceive and feel about our physical appearance — is shaped by a wide range of influences, including social media, cultural messages, and our own inner dialogue. It is entirely normal for these feelings to shift over time, and struggling with body image does not mean something is permanently wrong with you or your relationship with yourself.

One step that many people find helpful is becoming more aware of the language they use when thinking about their bodies. The internal voice we direct at ourselves can be harsh in ways we would never speak to a close friend. Practising a gentler, more neutral inner dialogue — noticing critical thoughts without treating them as facts — is something research consistently links to improved wellbeing and self-compassion over time.

Another approach worth considering is taking a mindful look at the media and social environments you spend time in. Curating your social media feed to include more diverse and realistic representations of bodies, or simply reducing screen time, is something a number of people find creates more mental breathing room. The images we absorb repeatedly can quietly shape how we evaluate ourselves, often without us realising it.

It can also be meaningful to gradually shift focus toward what your body allows you to do and experience, rather than solely how it appears. This is not about forced positivity, but about gently broadening the relationship you have with your physical self over time.

Struggling with body image is a common human experience, and it is one that many people find improves with time, reflection, and the right support. If these feelings are persistent or significantly affecting your daily life, speaking with a mental health professional can be a genuinely useful step forward.
