# Comparing yourself to others

Spending time comparing ourselves to others is something almost everyone experiences at some point. Whether it's scrolling through social media, sitting in a work meeting, or catching up with friends, it's natural for the mind to measure where we are against where someone else seems to be. While this tendency is very human, when it becomes a frequent habit it can quietly chip away at our sense of self-worth and wellbeing.

One thing that many people find helpful is noticing the difference between what they see of others and what is actually true. What we observe — especially online — is often a carefully selected highlight, not a full picture. Someone else's achievement or lifestyle rarely tells the whole story of their struggles, doubts, or setbacks. Gently reminding ourselves of this when comparisons arise can help loosen their grip.

Another strategy that may help is redirecting attention toward personal progress rather than external benchmarks. This is sometimes called moving from a "social comparison" mindset toward a "personal growth" mindset — measuring today's self against yesterday's, rather than against someone else's entirely different path. Keeping a simple note of small wins or moments of effort, however modest, can help build a more grounded sense of self over time.

It can also be worth exploring what a comparison is really pointing to. Sometimes a pang of envy or inadequacy is quietly signalling something we care about — a goal we haven't yet pursued, or a need that isn't being met. Approached with curiosity rather than self-criticism, these moments can offer useful information rather than just discomfort.

If comparing yourself to others is affecting your mood, relationships, or day-to-day life, you don't have to work through it alone. Support is available, and many people find that talking with a professional can make a real difference.
