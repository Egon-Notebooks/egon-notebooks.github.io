# Imposter syndrome

Feeling like a fraud despite clear evidence of your abilities is something many people experience — and it has a name. Imposter syndrome describes the internal sense that your achievements are undeserved, that you have somehow fooled the people around you, and that it is only a matter of time before you are "found out." It is more common than most people realize, affecting people across all kinds of careers, backgrounds, and life stages.

One thing that many people find helpful is simply naming the experience. When that inner voice suggests you do not belong or have not truly earned your place, recognizing it as imposter syndrome — rather than as objective truth — can create a small but meaningful distance between the thought and how you respond to it. Thoughts are not always facts, and noticing this pattern is often the first step toward loosening its grip.

Another strategy that some people find useful is keeping a record of their achievements, positive feedback, and moments where their skills made a genuine difference. Over time, this kind of evidence can offer a counterbalance to the mind's tendency to dismiss or minimize success. It does not need to be elaborate — even a few notes in a phone or notebook can be a grounding reminder when self-doubt feels loudest.

Talking with trusted colleagues, friends, or mentors can also help. Many people are surprised to discover that others — including those they admire — share similar feelings. This kind of honest conversation can reduce the isolation that imposter syndrome often thrives on.

It is worth remembering that experiencing self-doubt does not mean you are incapable. Many people find that with the right support — whether through conversation, self-reflection, or working with a mental health professional — these feelings become much easier to manage, and a more grounded sense of confidence can grow.
