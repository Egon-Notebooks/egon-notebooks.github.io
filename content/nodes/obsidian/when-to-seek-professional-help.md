# When to seek professional help

Knowing when to reach out for professional support is one of the most important — and sometimes one of the most difficult — decisions a person can make for their mental health. Many people wonder whether what they are experiencing is "serious enough" to warrant help. The truth is, there is no threshold you need to cross before your wellbeing deserves attention.

A useful starting point is to notice how your thoughts, feelings, or behaviours are affecting your daily life. If you find that low mood, persistent worry, difficulty sleeping, or emotional numbness has been present for two weeks or more, or if these experiences are getting in the way of work, relationships, or everyday activities you once managed comfortably, these can be meaningful signals worth paying attention to. You do not need to be in crisis for your experience to matter.

It can also help to check in with yourself about coping. Many people find that the strategies they usually rely on — talking to a friend, getting outside, resting — stop feeling effective during harder periods. When it feels difficult to manage day to day, or when you notice you are withdrawing from things and people that used to bring comfort, this may be a sign that additional support could make a real difference. Feeling as though your thoughts are overwhelming or hard to control is another experience that mental health professionals are well-equipped to help with.

Reaching out for support is a sign of self-awareness, not weakness. Mental health professionals are trained to help people navigate a wide range of experiences — from everyday stress to more complex challenges — and many people find that even a small number of sessions can shift how they feel and cope. Whatever you are going through, support is available, and things can change.
