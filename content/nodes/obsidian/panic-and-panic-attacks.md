# Panic and panic attacks

Panic attacks can feel overwhelming and frightening, but they are a well-understood experience that many people go through at some point in their lives. A panic attack is a sudden surge of intense fear or discomfort, often accompanied by physical sensations such as a racing heart, shortness of breath, dizziness, or a feeling of unreality. Although these experiences can feel alarming, they are not physically dangerous, and they do pass.

One strategy many people find helpful during a panic attack is focusing on slow, controlled breathing. When panic sets in, breathing often becomes rapid and shallow, which can intensify physical symptoms. Gently slowing the breath — for example, by breathing in for a count of four and out for a count of six — may help signal to the body that it is safe, allowing the intensity of the experience to ease over time.

Another approach that some people find useful is grounding — gently redirecting attention to the present moment and immediate surroundings. This might involve noticing five things you can see, four things you can feel against your skin, or simply the sensation of your feet on the floor. Grounding techniques work by engaging the senses and can help interrupt the cycle of escalating thoughts that often accompanies panic.

It can also be helpful to understand that avoiding situations associated with panic attacks, while understandable, can sometimes make anxiety stronger over time. Gradually and gently facing feared situations — ideally with professional support — is something many people find meaningful in their recovery.

If panic attacks are happening frequently or affecting your daily life, reaching out for support is a positive and courageous step. With the right help, many people learn to understand their panic responses, reduce their frequency, and regain a sense of confidence and calm. You do not have to navigate this alone.
