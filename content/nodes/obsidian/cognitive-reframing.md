# Cognitive reframing

Cognitive reframing is a technique rooted in cognitive-behavioural therapy that involves gently shifting the way we interpret a situation, thought, or experience. Rather than trying to think "positively" in a forced or unrealistic way, reframing invites us to consider whether there might be another, more balanced perspective available to us — one that is just as valid, but less distressing.

One practical way to begin exploring reframing is to notice and name an unhelpful thought when it arises. For example, a thought like "I always fail at everything" can be examined with curiosity rather than accepted as fact. Many people find it helpful to ask themselves: "Is this thought entirely true? Is there any evidence that contradicts it?" This gentle questioning can create a small but meaningful space between a difficult thought and an automatic emotional response.

Another approach involves looking for what might be called the broader view. When something goes wrong, our minds often narrow their focus onto the worst possible interpretation. Deliberately asking "What else could this mean?" or "How might I see this differently in a few weeks?" can help widen that lens. This does not mean dismissing real difficulties — it means holding them alongside a fuller picture of the situation.

It is also worth recognising that reframing is a skill, which means it takes practice and patience. Some people find it useful to work through this process in a journal, while others benefit from exploring it with a trusted friend or a mental health professional who can offer an outside perspective.

With time, many people find that reframing becomes a more natural habit of mind — one that supports greater emotional flexibility and resilience. If you feel that unhelpful thought patterns are significantly affecting your daily life, speaking with a mental health professional can make a real difference.
