# Recovering from burnout

Burnout is a state of deep exhaustion — emotional, physical, and mental — that can develop after a prolonged period of stress, overwork, or feeling chronically overwhelmed. It is a recognized and common experience, not a sign of weakness or failure. Many people move through periods of burnout and, with the right support and adjustments, find their way back to a greater sense of balance and wellbeing.

One of the most important steps in recovering from burnout is allowing genuine rest. This goes beyond simply taking a day off — it means creating space for activities that feel truly restorative, whether that is spending time in nature, connecting with people who feel supportive, or engaging in quiet, low-demand activities. Many people find that identifying what actually replenishes their energy, rather than what they think "should" relax them, makes a meaningful difference.

It can also help to gently examine the conditions that contributed to burnout in the first place. This is not about blame, but about understanding. Looking at boundaries around work, commitments, or relationships — and considering small, realistic adjustments — can help reduce the risk of the cycle repeating. Some people find it useful to work through this process with a therapist or counsellor, who can offer a supportive and objective perspective.

Building recovery gradually is key. Many people experiencing burnout put pressure on themselves to bounce back quickly, which can make things harder. Recognizing that recovery takes time, and that progress may feel slow or uneven, is part of the process. Small steps — like protecting sleep, reducing unnecessary demands, or simply acknowledging how you are feeling — can add up over time.

If burnout is significantly affecting your daily life, reaching out to a healthcare professional or mental health support is a worthwhile step. You do not have to navigate this alone, and support is available.
