# All-or-nothing thinking

All-or-nothing thinking — sometimes called black-and-white thinking — is a common thought pattern where situations, people, or even ourselves are seen in extremes, with no middle ground. Something is either a complete success or a total failure; a person is either entirely good or entirely bad. While this kind of thinking can feel very convincing in the moment, it often creates unnecessary distress and makes it harder to see situations clearly.

One of the most helpful first steps is simply learning to notice when all-or-nothing thinking is happening. Many people find it useful to pause and ask themselves: "Is there a more nuanced way to look at this?" For example, making a mistake at work doesn't mean an entire project is ruined, and having a difficult day doesn't mean every day will feel the same way. Catching the thought before it spirals can create a small but meaningful moment of perspective.

From there, some people find it helpful to practice looking for "the middle ground." This might mean rating situations on a scale rather than placing them in rigid categories, or deliberately listing evidence that challenges an extreme thought. Keeping a journal to track these moments can also make patterns easier to spot over time, which is something many people find grounding.

It's also worth recognizing that all-or-nothing thinking tends to intensify during periods of stress, low mood, or anxiety — so noticing this pattern can sometimes be a signal that something deeper may need attention.

With the right support, thought patterns like these can shift. Approaches such as cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) are specifically designed to help people examine and gently reshape unhelpful thinking habits. If you find that this pattern is getting in the way of your daily life or wellbeing, speaking with a mental health professional can be a meaningful and positive step forward.
