# Challenging unhelpful thoughts

Many of us experience moments when our thoughts feel overwhelming, critical, or stuck in a loop. These patterns — sometimes called unhelpful or distorted thinking — can affect how we feel and how we respond to situations. The encouraging news is that thoughts are not facts, and with practice, it is possible to develop a different relationship with them.

One approach that many people find useful is simply noticing a thought before reacting to it. When a difficult thought arises, pausing to ask "is this definitely true?" or "am I seeing the full picture?" can create a small but meaningful space between the thought and the feeling it produces. This is not about forcing positivity, but about gently questioning assumptions that may not be serving you.

Another strategy involves looking for evidence. If a thought says "I always get things wrong," it can help to consider moments that tell a different story — times when things did go well, or when effort led to progress. This is sometimes called "thought balancing," and it can gradually shift patterns that feel very fixed. Writing thoughts down, even briefly, can make this process feel more manageable and easier to reflect on.

It is also worth paying attention to the language thoughts use. Words like "never," "always," "everyone," and "worthless" tend to appear in unhelpful thinking patterns, and spotting them can be a signal to take a closer look. Some people find it helpful to consider what they might say to a trusted friend who was having the same thought — often, we extend more kindness to others than to ourselves.

Challenging unhelpful thoughts is a skill, and like any skill, it takes time to develop. Many people find that working with a trained professional can make the process feel more supported and less daunting. You do not have to navigate this alone, and support is available.
