# Personality and mental health

Personality shapes the way we see the world, relate to others, and respond to life's challenges. While it is not a fixed blueprint, our personality traits — the patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving that feel most natural to us — can have a meaningful influence on our mental health and emotional wellbeing.

Research suggests that certain personality traits are linked to how we experience and manage stress. For example, people who tend toward perfectionism or self-criticism may find themselves more vulnerable to anxiety or low mood when things don't go as planned. Similarly, those who find it difficult to trust others may struggle to build the social connections that can be so important for emotional resilience. Understanding these patterns is not about labeling ourselves, but about gaining insight that can support meaningful change.

One approach many people find helpful is simply noticing recurring patterns — moments when a reaction feels outsized, or a habit of thought seems to be working against you. Bringing gentle curiosity to these patterns, perhaps with the help of a journal or a trusted person in your life, can be a first step toward understanding them more clearly. Therapeutic approaches such as cognitive behavioral therapy or schema therapy are specifically designed to help people explore how deep-seated patterns of thought and behavior develop, and how they can shift over time.

It is also worth remembering that personality is not destiny. Many people find that with support, self-awareness, and time, the traits that once felt like obstacles become easier to work with — and sometimes even become sources of strength. If you feel that your personality patterns are affecting your mental health or quality of life, reaching out to a mental health professional can be a meaningful and worthwhile step. Support is available, and things can change.
