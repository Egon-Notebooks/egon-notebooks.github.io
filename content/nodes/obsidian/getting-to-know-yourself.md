# Getting to know yourself

Self-awareness — understanding your own thoughts, feelings, values, and patterns of behaviour — is one of the most quietly powerful things you can cultivate for your mental wellbeing. It doesn't require any special knowledge or experience to begin. It simply starts with paying a little more attention to what's happening inside you, with curiosity rather than judgment.

One place many people find helpful to start is reflective journaling. Setting aside even a few minutes to write about how your day felt, what made you react strongly, or what you found yourself avoiding can reveal patterns you might not notice in the rush of daily life. Over time, these small observations can offer meaningful insight into what matters to you and what might be weighing on you.

Another approach is practising mindful check-ins throughout the day. This means pausing briefly to notice your emotional state without immediately trying to fix or dismiss it. Asking yourself simple questions — "What am I feeling right now?", "What do I need in this moment?" — can build a habit of gentle self-inquiry. Many people find that this kind of regular check-in helps them feel more grounded and less caught off guard by their own emotions.

It can also be valuable to pay attention to your personal values — the things that feel genuinely important to you, separate from what others expect. When your daily choices feel misaligned with your values, it can quietly contribute to low mood or a sense of dissatisfaction. Reflecting on what truly matters to you can help guide more fulfilling decisions over time.

Getting to know yourself is an ongoing journey, not a destination, and it looks different for everyone. If you find that self-reflection brings up difficult feelings that feel hard to manage alone, speaking with a mental health professional can provide a safe and supportive space to explore them further.
