# Trauma responses and the nervous system

Experiencing something frightening, overwhelming, or deeply distressing can leave a lasting imprint — not just on our thoughts and emotions, but on our bodies too. Understanding how trauma affects the nervous system can be a meaningful step toward self-compassion and healing.

When a person goes through a traumatic experience, the body's survival system activates automatically. The nervous system — particularly a network known as the autonomic nervous system — responds to perceived danger by shifting into protective states. Many people are familiar with the "fight or flight" response, where the body mobilizes energy to confront or escape a threat. Less commonly discussed is the "freeze" response, where the body becomes still or shut down. These are not choices; they are automatic, biological reactions designed to keep us safe. Understanding this can help ease any shame or confusion a person might feel about how they responded during or after a difficult event.

After trauma, the nervous system can sometimes remain in a heightened state of alertness, even when the danger has passed. This may show up as feeling easily startled, having difficulty sleeping, feeling emotionally numb, or experiencing sudden waves of anxiety or distress. Some people find that certain sounds, smells, or situations seem to bring the feelings of the original event rushing back. These experiences make sense in the context of how the nervous system works — it has learned to scan for danger in order to protect us.

Many people find that gentle, body-focused practices can support nervous system regulation over time. Slow, intentional breathing, spending time in nature, mindful movement such as yoga or walking, and safe social connection are all approaches that research suggests may help the body gradually find a greater sense of calm.

Trauma responses are a natural reaction to extraordinary circumstances — they are not a sign of weakness or permanent damage. With the right support, many people find that healing is possible, and that their nervous system can learn, over time, to feel safer again.
