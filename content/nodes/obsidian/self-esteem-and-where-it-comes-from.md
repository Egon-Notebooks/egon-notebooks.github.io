# Self-esteem and where it comes from

Self-esteem is often described as the overall sense of worth or value we hold about ourselves. It shapes how we relate to others, how we respond to challenges, and how we speak to ourselves in quiet moments. Understanding where self-esteem comes from can be a meaningful first step toward building a healthier relationship with yourself.

Self-esteem develops over time and is shaped by many influences. Early experiences — including relationships with caregivers, feedback from peers, and messages absorbed from school and culture — can leave a lasting impression on how we see ourselves. This does not mean those early experiences have the final word. The brain remains adaptable throughout life, and the stories we tell ourselves about who we are can shift and grow.

One factor that many people find helpful is becoming more aware of their inner dialogue — the way they talk to themselves throughout the day. Harsh, critical self-talk often feels automatic, but with practice, it is possible to notice these patterns and gently challenge them. Some people find it useful to ask whether they would speak to a close friend the same way they speak to themselves. This simple shift in perspective can begin to loosen the grip of long-held self-critical beliefs.

Meaningful connection with others also plays an important role. Relationships in which we feel genuinely seen and accepted can, over time, help to counteract negative self-perceptions. Engaging in activities that align with personal values — rather than seeking external validation — can also nurture a more stable and authentic sense of self-worth.

It is worth remembering that self-esteem is not a fixed trait. It can change, and many people have found that with time, support, and self-compassion, their relationship with themselves has improved. If self-esteem is something you are struggling with, reaching out to a mental health professional is a meaningful and worthwhile step.
