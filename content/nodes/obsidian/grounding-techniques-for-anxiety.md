# Grounding techniques for anxiety

Anxiety can sometimes feel like being swept up in a current — thoughts racing, heart pounding, the present moment slipping out of reach. Grounding techniques are simple, practical tools that can help bring attention back to the here and now, gently interrupting the cycle of anxious thinking. Many people find that with regular practice, these techniques become easier to use in moments when anxiety feels overwhelming.

One widely used approach is the 5-4-3-2-1 technique, which invites you to notice five things you can see, four things you can physically feel, three things you can hear, two things you can smell, and one thing you can taste. By deliberately engaging the senses, this exercise encourages the mind to reconnect with the present environment rather than staying caught in worried thoughts. It can be done quietly and discreetly almost anywhere.

Slow, intentional breathing is another strategy many people find helpful. Anxiety often causes breathing to become shallow and rapid, which can heighten feelings of unease. Deliberately slowing the breath — for example, breathing in for a count of four, holding briefly, and breathing out for a count of six — can help activate the body's natural calming response. Even a few minutes of focused breathing may begin to ease the physical sensations of anxiety.

Physical grounding can also be effective. Pressing your feet firmly into the floor, holding a cool glass of water, or placing a hand over your heart are small actions that draw awareness back into the body and away from anxious thoughts. These approaches are rooted in the understanding that reconnecting with physical sensations can help settle an activated nervous system.

It is worth remembering that anxiety is a very common human experience, and support is available. Speaking with a healthcare professional or mental health practitioner can make a meaningful difference, and many people find that things do improve with time and the right help.
