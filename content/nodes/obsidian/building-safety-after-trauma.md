# Building safety after trauma

Experiencing trauma can leave a person feeling unsafe in their own body, their relationships, or the world around them. This sense of danger — even when the immediate threat has passed — is a natural response to overwhelming experiences. The good news is that safety is something that can be gradually rebuilt, at a pace that feels right for each individual.

One of the first steps many people find helpful is identifying what already feels safe. This might be a particular room, a time of day, a trusted person, or even a simple routine like making a morning cup of tea. Noticing these existing anchors, however small, can provide a foundation to build from. Safety does not have to be created from nothing — it is often already present in small moments waiting to be recognized.

Grounding practices are another tool that many people find supportive. These are simple techniques that gently bring attention back to the present moment — such as noticing five things you can see around you, or feeling the weight of your feet on the floor. For people who have experienced trauma, the mind can sometimes feel pulled back into difficult memories. Grounding can help create a sense of being here, now, in a body that is no longer in danger.

It can also help to slowly and intentionally expand comfort zones over time. This does not mean pushing through distress, but rather taking small, chosen steps — perhaps spending a little longer in a space that once felt uncomfortable, or sharing something small with a trusted person. Many people find that each small step, however modest, can contribute to a growing sense of confidence and safety.

Rebuilding safety after trauma is a gradual process, and it rarely looks the same for two people. Support from a trained professional can make a meaningful difference in this journey, offering guidance that is tailored to your unique experience. Reaching out for help is a sign of strength, and things can genuinely get better with time and care.
