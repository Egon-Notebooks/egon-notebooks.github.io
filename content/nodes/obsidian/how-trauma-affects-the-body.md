# How trauma affects the body

Trauma is not only an experience that lives in memory — it can also leave a lasting impression on the body. Many people who have been through distressing events notice physical symptoms that seem disconnected from what happened to them, such as tension, fatigue, difficulty sleeping, or a heightened sense of alertness. Understanding why this happens can be a meaningful first step toward healing.

When a person experiences something overwhelming, the body's survival systems respond automatically. The nervous system shifts into a state of protection — often described as "fight, flight, or freeze." This is a healthy, natural response to danger. However, when the threat has passed but the body hasn't had the chance to fully process what happened, it can remain in a kind of alert state. Over time, this may contribute to physical sensations like a racing heart, shallow breathing, chronic muscle tension, or feeling emotionally "numb." Some people find that certain sights, sounds, or situations can bring these feelings rushing back without warning.

There are approaches that many people find helpful in supporting the body through trauma recovery. Gentle, body-aware practices such as mindful breathing, yoga, or progressive muscle relaxation may help the nervous system gradually settle. Some people find that creative expression — through movement, art, or writing — offers a way to process difficult experiences that words alone cannot reach. Building a sense of safety in everyday routines and relationships can also support the body's gradual return to a calmer baseline.

It is worth remembering that these responses are not signs of weakness — they are signs that your body tried to protect you. Recovery looks different for everyone, and it often unfolds over time with the right support. Speaking with a trained professional can make a real difference, and reaching out is a courageous and worthwhile step.
