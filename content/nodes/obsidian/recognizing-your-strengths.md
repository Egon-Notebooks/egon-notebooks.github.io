# Recognizing your strengths

Focusing on what is going wrong can feel automatic when life becomes difficult. Yet most people carry a quiet store of personal strengths — qualities, skills, and experiences that have helped them navigate challenges before and can do so again. Learning to recognize those strengths is not about ignoring difficulties; it is about building a more complete and honest picture of who you are.

One place to start is by reflecting on past experiences. Think of a time you managed something hard — even something that may seem small in hindsight. What did you draw on to get through it? Patience, persistence, creativity, the ability to ask for help — these are all genuine strengths. Many people find it useful to write these reflections down, as seeing them on paper can make them feel more real and easier to return to on harder days.

Another approach is to pay attention to what others appreciate about you. Feedback from people who know you well — a friend, a colleague, a family member — can reveal qualities you may have stopped noticing in yourself. This is not about seeking validation, but about gathering evidence that your perspective on yourself may be incomplete. Strengths often live in our blind spots.

It can also help to reframe what you consider a strength. Sensitivity, for example, is sometimes experienced as a burden, yet it often comes with deep empathy and the ability to connect meaningfully with others. Looking at your qualities from a different angle can shift how you relate to them.

Recognizing your strengths is a skill that develops over time, and it is one that many people find easier to build with support. Talking with a mental health professional can offer a supportive space to explore your sense of self and discover resources you may not have known you had.
