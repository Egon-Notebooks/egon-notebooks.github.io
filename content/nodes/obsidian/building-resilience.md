# Building resilience

Resilience is often described as the ability to adapt and recover in the face of difficulty — but it is not something people either have or don't have. It is a skill, and like any skill, it can be learned and strengthened over time. Understanding this can itself be a source of hope when life feels overwhelming.

One of the most well-supported ways to build resilience is through nurturing connection with others. Strong, trusting relationships — whether with friends, family, colleagues, or a community group — can provide a sense of belonging and a buffer against stress. This does not mean needing a large social network; even one or two meaningful relationships can make a significant difference. Many people find that reaching out, even briefly, during difficult times helps them feel less alone and more able to cope.

Another foundation of resilience is developing a compassionate relationship with yourself. This means recognizing that struggling is a normal part of being human, not a sign of weakness or failure. Some people find that practices such as mindfulness, journaling, or simply pausing to notice their own emotions without judgment can help them respond to challenges more thoughtfully, rather than feeling overwhelmed by them. Building routines that support physical wellbeing — such as regular movement, rest, and time outdoors — can also contribute to emotional steadiness over time.

A third element involves finding meaning or purpose, even in small things. This might be a creative interest, a sense of contribution to others, or personal values that guide decision-making. Having something to orient toward can help people navigate uncertainty with greater stability.

Resilience does not mean avoiding pain or hardship — it means developing the inner and outer resources to move through it. Support from a mental health professional can be a valuable part of building those resources, and there is real strength in reaching out.
