# Attachment styles

Attachment styles describe the patterns we develop in how we relate to other people — particularly in close relationships. These patterns often take shape early in life, influenced by our experiences with caregivers, but they continue to show up in friendships, romantic relationships, and even the way we relate to ourselves as adults. Understanding your attachment style is not about assigning blame or labels — it is simply a way of gaining insight into why you might feel, think, or behave the way you do in relationships.

Researchers generally describe four broad attachment styles: secure, anxious, avoidant, and disorganized. People with a secure attachment style tend to feel comfortable with closeness and are generally able to trust others. Those with an anxious style may worry frequently about whether they are loved or valued. People with an avoidant style often find emotional intimacy uncomfortable and may pull back when relationships feel too close. Disorganized attachment can involve elements of both anxiety and avoidance, sometimes making relationships feel confusing or unpredictable. Most people find that their experiences do not fit perfectly into one category, and that is entirely normal.

The encouraging truth is that attachment styles are not fixed. Many people find that developing self-awareness around their patterns is a meaningful first step. Some strategies that may help include practising self-compassion when old patterns emerge, reflecting on what feels safe or unsafe in relationships, and gradually building trust through small, consistent interactions with people you feel comfortable around. Keeping a journal or speaking with a trusted friend can also help some people begin to notice their patterns more clearly.

If you recognise yourself in any of these descriptions, you are not alone — and things can change. Working with a therapist or counsellor who understands attachment can be a valuable part of exploring these patterns and building the kinds of relationships that feel nurturing and secure.
