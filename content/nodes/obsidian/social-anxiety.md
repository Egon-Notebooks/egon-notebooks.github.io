# Social anxiety

Social anxiety is something many people experience — that uncomfortable sense of dread or self-consciousness in social situations, whether it's speaking up in a meeting, meeting new people, or even making a phone call. It goes beyond shyness. For some people, fear of being judged, embarrassed, or rejected can make everyday interactions feel genuinely overwhelming. Recognising that this experience is common, and that it doesn't have to stay this way, is an important first step.

One approach that many people find helpful is gradually and gently exposing themselves to situations they find difficult, rather than avoiding them entirely. Avoidance can feel like relief in the short term, but over time it often reinforces anxiety. Starting small — perhaps saying hello to a neighbour or joining a low-pressure group activity — can help build confidence in manageable steps. This kind of gradual approach is at the heart of evidence-based therapies for social anxiety.

Alongside this, many people benefit from learning to notice and gently question the thoughts that arise in social situations. Social anxiety often comes with a strong inner critic — a voice that predicts the worst or magnifies perceived mistakes. With practice, it's possible to recognise these thought patterns and respond to them with a little more kindness and perspective. Mindfulness-based approaches can also help by encouraging people to stay grounded in the present moment rather than caught in worry about how they are being perceived.

It is worth knowing that social anxiety responds well to support, and many people find that things genuinely improve with the right help. Whether that means speaking with a GP, working with a therapist, or connecting with others who share similar experiences, reaching out is a meaningful and courageous step. You don't have to navigate this alone.
