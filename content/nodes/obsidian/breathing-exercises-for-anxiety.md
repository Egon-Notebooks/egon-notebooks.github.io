# Breathing exercises for anxiety

Anxiety can make the body feel like it is in a state of constant alert — heart racing, muscles tense, breath shallow and quick. One of the simplest and most accessible ways to gently interrupt this response is through intentional breathing. Because breathing is one of the few automatic body functions we can consciously control, it offers a direct pathway to calming the nervous system.

One approach that many people find helpful is called diaphragmatic breathing, sometimes known as belly breathing. Rather than breathing shallowly into the chest, this technique involves breathing slowly and deeply so that the stomach rises and falls with each breath. Practicing this for even a few minutes can signal to the body that it is safe to relax, helping to ease the physical sensations that often accompany anxiety.

Another technique worth exploring is box breathing, which involves breathing in for a count of four, holding for four, breathing out for four, and holding again for four before repeating the cycle. This structured rhythm gives the mind something steady to focus on, which many people find grounding during moments of heightened worry or stress. Similarly, extended exhale breathing — where the out-breath is made longer than the in-breath — is supported by research suggesting it can help activate the body's natural calming response.

Like any skill, breathing exercises tend to become more effective with regular practice. Many people find it helpful to try them during calm moments first, so the techniques feel more familiar when anxiety arises. It is also worth remembering that breathing exercises are one tool among many — they work well alongside other forms of support, including talking to a mental health professional. If anxiety is significantly affecting your daily life, reaching out for guidance is a meaningful and worthwhile step.
