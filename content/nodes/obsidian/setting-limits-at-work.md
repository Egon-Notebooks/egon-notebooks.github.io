# Setting limits at work

Knowing when and how to set limits at work is something many people quietly struggle with. Whether it's taking on too many tasks, feeling unable to say no to a manager, or finding that work bleeds into personal time, the pressure to always be available can take a real toll on wellbeing. The good news is that setting limits — often called boundaries — is a skill that can be learned and practiced over time.

One place to start is building awareness of where your energy is going. Many people find it helpful to notice which situations consistently leave them feeling drained, resentful, or overwhelmed. These moments can act as useful signals, pointing toward areas where a limit might be needed. Keeping a simple journal or just pausing briefly at the end of each day to check in with yourself can support this kind of reflection.

Once you have a clearer picture, communicating limits to colleagues or managers can feel daunting, but small steps often make it more manageable. Some people find it useful to practice what they want to say in advance, start with lower-stakes situations, or frame their needs around the quality of their work — for example, explaining that focused time helps them perform better. It is also worth remembering that protecting your capacity is not selfish; it can help you show up more consistently and sustainably in the long run.

It is equally important to honour the limits you set. This might mean turning off work notifications outside of agreed hours, or resisting the urge to respond to non-urgent messages immediately. Small, consistent actions tend to reinforce new patterns over time.

If setting limits at work feels persistently difficult or is connected to deeper feelings of anxiety, low self-worth, or burnout, speaking with a mental health professional can offer meaningful support. Many people find that working through these challenges with guidance makes a real and lasting difference.
