# Understanding your triggers

Certain situations, words, sensations, or memories can sometimes spark an intense emotional reaction that feels overwhelming or hard to explain. These are often called triggers — moments where something in the present connects, consciously or not, to a past experience or an unmet need. Understanding your own triggers is not about labeling yourself or finding fault; it is about building self-awareness so that you can respond to difficult moments with greater care and intention.

One approach many people find helpful is simply noticing patterns over time. Keeping a brief journal after moments of strong emotional reaction — noting what happened, where you were, who was present, and what you felt in your body — can gradually reveal connections that were not obvious at first. You do not need to analyze or judge what you write; just observing can be a valuable first step.

Another strategy is learning to pause before reacting. When a trigger arises, the nervous system can shift into a stress response quickly. Some people find that slowing down the breath, grounding themselves in their physical surroundings, or simply naming what they are feeling ("I notice I feel anxious right now") can create a small but meaningful space between the trigger and the response. Over time, that space tends to grow.

It is also worth remembering that having triggers is a deeply human experience, not a sign of weakness or fragility. They often point to things that matter — relationships, safety, self-worth — and exploring them with curiosity rather than criticism can lead to genuine insight and growth.

If your triggers are significantly affecting your daily life or relationships, speaking with a mental health professional can make a real difference. With the right support, many people develop a much stronger sense of understanding and agency over their emotional responses.
