- Balancing the demands of work with personal wellbeing is something many people find genuinely difficult. Whether it's responding to emails late at night, saying yes when you mean no, or feeling unable to step away from your desk, the pressure to always be available can quietly take a toll on mental health over time. Learning to set limits around work is not a sign of weakness or lack of commitment — it is a practical and important part of sustaining your health and your ability to do good work.
- One approach many people find helpful is getting clear on what feels manageable before agreeing to new tasks or responsibilities. This might involve pausing before responding to a request, checking in with your current workload, and communicating honestly about capacity. Saying something like "I want to make sure I can do this well — can we talk about timelines?" is a way of being both professional and honest about your limits.
- Another strategy is creating clear transitions between work and rest. Many people find that small, consistent rituals — such as closing certain apps at a set time, going for a short walk after finishing for the day, or changing out of work clothes — can help signal to the mind that the working day has ended. These kinds of boundaries between work time and personal time can support better sleep, rest, and recovery.
- It is also worth recognising that persistent difficulty with workload, stress, or feelings of overwhelm may be worth talking about with someone you trust — whether that is a manager, a colleague, or a professional. These feelings are more common than many people realise, and support is available.
- Protecting your wellbeing at work is not selfish. For many people, it is one of the most important steps they can take.
