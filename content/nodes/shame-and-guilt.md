- Shame and guilt are emotions that almost everyone encounters at some point in life. While they can feel overwhelming or isolating, understanding the difference between them — and knowing that support is available — can be an important first step toward feeling better.
- Guilt and shame are closely related but are not the same thing. Guilt tends to focus on a specific action: "I did something wrong." Shame reaches deeper, targeting the sense of self: "I am wrong, or bad, or not enough." Guilt, in modest amounts, can actually be a healthy signal — it helps people recognise when their actions have moved away from their values, and it can motivate repair and change. Shame, on the other hand, often causes people to withdraw, hide, or feel fundamentally flawed. Over time, carrying a heavy burden of shame can contribute to low self-esteem, anxiety, and difficulties in relationships.
- One approach that many people find helpful is learning to separate their actions from their worth as a person. Making mistakes is part of being human — it does not define who someone is. Practising self-compassion, which involves treating oneself with the same kindness one might offer a friend, can gently loosen shame's grip over time. Research also suggests that sharing feelings of shame with a trusted, non-judgmental person — whether a friend, family member, or professional — can significantly reduce its intensity. Shame tends to grow in silence and secrecy, and connection can be a powerful counterforce.
- If feelings of shame or guilt feel persistent, intense, or are getting in the way of daily life, reaching out for support is a meaningful and courageous step. A mental health professional can help explore where these feelings come from and offer practical tools for working through them. Things can, and do, change — and no one has to navigate these emotions alone.
