- Feeling nervous in social situations is something most people experience from time to time. But for some people, that nervousness goes much deeper — bringing intense fear, self-consciousness, or dread around everyday interactions like speaking in a meeting, meeting new people, or even eating in public. This is social anxiety, and it is one of the most common mental health experiences in the world. If this sounds familiar, it is important to know that you are not alone, and that things can genuinely change.
- One of the most well-supported approaches for social anxiety is gradually and gently facing the situations that feel difficult, rather than avoiding them entirely. Avoidance can feel like relief in the short term, but over time it tends to reinforce the fear. Many people find that working with a therapist — particularly through a talking therapy called cognitive behavioural therapy, or CBT — helps them understand the thoughts driving their anxiety and build confidence in manageable steps.
- Alongside professional support, some everyday practices may also help. Many people find that slowing down the breath — breathing in slowly and out even more slowly — can calm the body's alarm response in anxious moments. Shifting attention outward, noticing the world around you rather than monitoring yourself, is another approach that some find useful. Connecting with others in low-pressure settings, even briefly, can also help rebuild a sense of ease around social situations over time.
- It is worth remembering that social anxiety does not define who you are or how your life will unfold. Many people who have experienced significant social anxiety have gone on to build rich, connected lives — often with the right support alongside them. Reaching out to a GP, counsellor, or mental health professional is a meaningful first step, and one that many people find makes a real difference.
