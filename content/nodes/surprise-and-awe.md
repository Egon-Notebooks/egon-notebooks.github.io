- Surprise and awe are two of the most quietly powerful experiences available to us. Whether it is the unexpected kindness of a stranger, a sudden view from a hilltop, or the moment a piece of music catches you off guard, these feelings have a way of pulling us out of our usual patterns of thought and reminding us that the world is larger than our worries. Researchers have begun to explore how these experiences affect our wellbeing — and the findings are genuinely encouraging.
- One area of growing interest is how awe, in particular, may help reduce what psychologists call "self-referential thinking" — the mental loop of rumination and self-criticism that can accompany anxiety and low mood. When we encounter something vast or unexpected, our attention shifts outward. Many people find that even small doses of awe, such as stargazing, visiting a place of natural beauty, or exploring an unfamiliar neighbourhood, can offer a gentle but meaningful shift in perspective.
- Cultivating openness to surprise is another approach some people find helpful. This does not require dramatic changes — it might be as simple as choosing a different route, trying a new food, or listening to music outside your usual taste. The goal is less about novelty for its own sake and more about gently loosening the grip of routine thinking, creating small moments where wonder can enter.
- It can also help to reflect on awe-inspiring experiences after the fact — writing briefly about them, sharing them with someone, or simply pausing to sit with the feeling. This kind of mindful attention may help extend the emotional benefit of these moments.
- If you are finding it difficult to feel present or connected to the world around you, you are not alone, and support is available. Talking with a mental health professional can open new ways of engaging with everyday life — and with yourself.
