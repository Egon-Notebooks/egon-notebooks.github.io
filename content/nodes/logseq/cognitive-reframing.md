- Cognitive reframing is a mental health technique that involves shifting the way we interpret situations, thoughts, or feelings. Rather than accepting a first, often harsh reaction to a difficult moment, reframing invites us to pause and consider whether there might be another, more balanced way to understand what is happening. It is a skill used in many forms of therapy and is something many people can begin to practise in everyday life.
- One of the most common ways to begin exploring reframing is to notice the language we use when things go wrong. When a mistake happens, thoughts like "I always fail" or "I'm terrible at this" can feel very convincing in the moment. Gently questioning these thoughts — asking "Is this always true?" or "What would I say to a friend in this situation?" — can create a small but meaningful shift in perspective. Over time, many people find that this kind of gentle questioning becomes more natural and less effortful.
- Another approach involves looking for what psychologists sometimes call "alternative explanations." When a difficult situation arises, there are often several possible ways to understand it. A colleague who seems distant might be preoccupied with something in their own life, rather than unhappy with you. Holding this possibility open does not mean dismissing your feelings — it means allowing more than one interpretation to exist at the same time.
- It is worth noting that reframing is not about forcing positivity or pretending things are fine when they are not. The goal is greater flexibility in thinking, not the suppression of genuine emotion.
- Learning to reframe thoughts is a gradual process, and it can be easier with support. Working with a mental health professional is one way many people develop this skill in a safe and guided way. Reaching out for that kind of support is always a reasonable and worthwhile step.
