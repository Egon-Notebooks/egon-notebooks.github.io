- Cognitive behavioral therapy, often called CBT, is one of the most widely researched and used approaches in mental health care. At its core, CBT is based on the idea that our thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are closely connected — and that by gently shifting unhelpful thinking patterns, it's possible to experience meaningful changes in how we feel and act day to day.
- One of the foundational principles of CBT is learning to notice automatic thoughts — the quick, often unconscious judgments we make about ourselves, others, or situations. Many people find that these thoughts are not entirely accurate, and that examining them with curiosity rather than judgment can reduce their emotional impact. A therapist trained in CBT can guide someone through this process in a structured, collaborative way.
- CBT also focuses on behavior change alongside thought patterns. Some strategies that may help include gradually reintroducing activities that bring a sense of accomplishment or connection, or practicing responses to situations that typically feel overwhelming. Over time, these small behavioral shifts can reinforce a more balanced way of thinking and help build confidence in managing difficult emotions.
- Another strength of CBT is that it tends to be goal-oriented and practical. Many people work with a therapist over a defined number of sessions, developing skills they can continue to use independently. This means the benefits of CBT can extend well beyond the therapy room, offering tools that support long-term wellbeing.
- CBT is used to support people experiencing a wide range of challenges, including [[Understanding anxiety|anxiety]], depression, and [[Understanding stress|stress]]. If you're curious about whether this approach might be a good fit, speaking with a mental health professional is a valuable first step. Support is available, and many people find that exploring the right therapeutic approach opens up a meaningful path forward.
