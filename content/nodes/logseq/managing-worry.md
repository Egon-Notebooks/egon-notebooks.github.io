- Worry is a natural part of being human. Most people experience it at some point — that restless loop of "what if" thoughts that can make even ordinary days feel heavy. While some degree of worry can be useful, helping us prepare for challenges ahead, it can also become overwhelming when it takes up a lot of mental space. The good news is that there are practical ways to work with worry, rather than feeling controlled by it.
- One approach many people find helpful is setting aside a specific "worry time" each day — perhaps ten or fifteen minutes where they allow themselves to think through their concerns deliberately. When worries arise outside of that window, they gently remind themselves to return to those thoughts later. This can help prevent worry from spreading throughout the whole day and may make it feel more manageable over time.
- Another strategy involves noticing the difference between worries we can act on and those we cannot. For concerns within our control, breaking the situation into small, concrete steps can ease the sense of helplessness that often accompanies worry. For things outside our control, practices like slow, intentional breathing or grounding exercises — such as noticing five things you can see or hear around you — may help settle the nervous system in moments of heightened anxiety.
- It can also make a difference to talk about what you are experiencing. Sharing worries with a trusted person — a friend, family member, or professional — can reduce their intensity and help you feel less alone with your thoughts.
- If worry is interfering with your daily life, sleep, or relationships, reaching out for support is a meaningful and courageous step. Many people find that with the right support, worry becomes something they can navigate rather than something that defines their days.
