- Social anxiety is more than just shyness or nerves before a big event. For many people, it involves an intense, persistent worry about being judged, embarrassed, or rejected in social situations — and it can make everyday interactions feel genuinely overwhelming. It is one of the most common mental health experiences, and for those living with it, the impact on daily life can be significant. The important thing to know is that social anxiety is well understood, and many people find meaningful relief with the right support.
- One strategy that many people find helpful is gently and gradually facing situations that feel uncomfortable, rather than avoiding them entirely. Avoidance can feel like relief in the short term, but over time it tends to reinforce anxiety. Taking small, manageable steps — such as making brief eye contact, exchanging a few words with a neighbour, or joining a low-pressure group activity — can slowly build confidence in social settings.
- Another approach that has strong evidence behind it is learning to notice and gently challenge unhelpful thought patterns. Social anxiety often involves a tendency to overestimate how negatively others are judging us, or to replay social moments and focus on perceived mistakes. Practising a more balanced, compassionate inner voice — the kind you might use with a good friend — can gradually shift how social situations feel. Some people find it useful to work through these techniques with a therapist trained in cognitive behavioural approaches, though there are also self-guided resources that many find accessible and helpful.
- Taking care of the basics — sleep, physical movement, and connection with people who feel safe — can also support overall wellbeing alongside more targeted strategies.
- If social anxiety is getting in the way of the life you want to live, reaching out for support is a meaningful and courageous step. Many people find that things do improve, and you do not have to work through it alone.
