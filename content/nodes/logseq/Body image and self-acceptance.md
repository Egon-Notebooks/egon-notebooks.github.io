- Feeling at peace with your body can be one of the most challenging aspects of emotional wellbeing — and one of the most meaningful. Body image refers to how we see, think, and feel about our physical appearance, and it can shift from day to day, often shaped by past experiences, cultural messages, and the images we encounter in media and social environments. Struggling with body image is incredibly common, and it says nothing about a person's worth or character.
- One approach many people find helpful is practicing mindful awareness of critical self-talk. When a negative thought about your body arises, noticing it — rather than automatically accepting it as truth — can create a small but important moment of distance. Some people find it useful to ask: "Would I speak to a friend this way?" That question alone can gently shift perspective over time.
- Another strategy that may support self-acceptance is focusing on what the body can do, rather than how it looks. This might mean appreciating physical sensations like warmth, movement, or rest, or acknowledging the ways your body carries you through daily life. This is sometimes called a functional appreciation approach, and some research suggests it can help reduce appearance-based dissatisfaction over time.
- Reducing or adjusting time spent on social media platforms — particularly those heavy with idealized or edited images — is something many people find supportive as well. This doesn't mean avoiding these spaces entirely, but becoming more intentional about them can protect emotional energy.
- Body image concerns exist on a wide spectrum, and for some people they can feel deeply distressing and persistent. If thoughts about your appearance are affecting your daily life, relationships, or sense of self, reaching out to a mental health professional can be a meaningful step. Support is available, and things can change.
