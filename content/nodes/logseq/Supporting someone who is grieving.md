- [[Understanding grief|Grief]] is one of the most profound human experiences, and knowing how to support someone going through it can feel uncertain or even overwhelming. There is no perfect thing to say or do — but showing up with care and consistency can make a meaningful difference to someone navigating loss.
- One of the most helpful things you can do is simply be present without trying to fix anything. Many people feel pressure to offer solutions or reassurance, but [[Understanding grief|grief]] is not a problem to be solved. Sitting with someone, listening without judgment, and acknowledging their pain — even with something as simple as "I'm so sorry, I'm here" — can provide real comfort. Resist the urge to minimize the loss or suggest timelines for how long [[Understanding grief|grief]] should last, as these responses, however well-intentioned, can leave a grieving person feeling unheard.
- Practical support often matters more than people expect. In the early days of loss especially, everyday tasks can feel impossible. Offering specific help — such as preparing a meal, handling an errand, or checking in regularly over the following weeks — tends to be more useful than a general "let me know if you need anything." [[Understanding grief|Grief]] also does not follow a short schedule, and reaching out weeks or months later can remind someone that they have not been forgotten.
- Taking care of your own emotional wellbeing matters too. Supporting someone in [[Understanding grief|grief]] can be heavy, and it is okay to acknowledge that. Seeking your own support, whether through a trusted friend or a professional, helps you stay present for the person you care about.
- There is no single right way to grieve, and no single right way to offer support. What matters most is that the person knows they are not alone. If you or someone you love is struggling with [[Understanding grief|grief]], professional support is available and can help.
