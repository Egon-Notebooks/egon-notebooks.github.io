- Group therapy is a form of psychotherapy where a small number of people meet together, guided by one or more trained therapists, to share experiences and support one another. For many people, the idea of opening up in front of others can feel daunting at first — but group therapy has a strong evidence base and offers something that individual therapy cannot always replicate: the experience of being truly understood by people who have faced similar challenges.
- One of the most powerful elements of group therapy is the sense of connection it can create. Many people who struggle with their mental health carry a quiet belief that they are uniquely broken or alone in what they are feeling. Hearing others describe experiences that feel remarkably familiar can gently challenge that belief, and many people find this sense of shared humanity deeply comforting and validating.
- Group therapy also offers a space to practice real-world skills in a supported environment. Whether the group is focused on managing [[Understanding anxiety|anxiety]], processing [[Understanding grief|grief]], building communication skills, or something else entirely, participants have the opportunity to give and receive feedback, try new ways of relating, and notice their own patterns within a safe, structured setting. Giving support to others can itself be a meaningful and affirming experience.
- Groups vary widely in their format, focus, and length — some are short-term and skills-based, while others are longer-term and more exploratory. A mental health professional can help someone decide whether group therapy might be a good fit, either on its own or alongside individual support.
- It is completely normal to feel uncertain about whether group therapy is right for you. Many people who were initially hesitant have found it to be a meaningful part of their journey toward feeling better. Support is available, and taking even a small step toward it can make a difference.
