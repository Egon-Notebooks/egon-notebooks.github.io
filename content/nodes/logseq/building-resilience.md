- Resilience is often described as the ability to adapt and recover when life becomes difficult. It is not a fixed trait that some people are born with and others are not — rather, it is something that can be nurtured and strengthened over time, even in the midst of challenging circumstances.
- One of the most well-supported ways to build resilience is through connection. Maintaining relationships with people who offer understanding and encouragement — whether friends, family members, or community groups — can provide a sense of stability when things feel uncertain. Many people find that simply sharing what they are going through with someone they trust makes difficulties feel more manageable.
- Another important element is developing a relationship with your own thoughts and emotions. Practices such as mindfulness, journaling, or even taking a few quiet moments in the day can help people become more aware of how they are feeling without being overwhelmed by those feelings. Over time, this kind of self-awareness can make it easier to respond to stress thoughtfully rather than reactively. Similarly, small, consistent actions — maintaining a routine, getting regular sleep, or spending time outdoors — can help create a sense of groundedness that supports emotional recovery.
- It also helps to reflect on past experiences where you have coped with difficulty. Many people find that looking back on challenges they have navigated — even imperfectly — reminds them that they have more inner strength than they may realise in a hard moment. Resilience is not about avoiding pain or always feeling strong; it is about continuing to move forward, even gradually, through difficulty.
- If you are finding it hard to cope, reaching out for professional support is a meaningful and courageous step. Nobody has to face their struggles alone, and support is available.
