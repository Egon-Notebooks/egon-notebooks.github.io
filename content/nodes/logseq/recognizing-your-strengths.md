- Recognizing your own strengths can feel surprisingly difficult, especially during times of stress or low mood. Yet understanding what we do well — and what makes us resilient — is a meaningful part of supporting our own mental wellbeing. Strengths are not just about talent or achievement; they can include qualities like patience, curiosity, loyalty, or the ability to listen well.
- One way many people find helpful is to reflect on moments when they felt effective, engaged, or proud — however small those moments might seem. Keeping a simple journal where you note one thing you handled well each day can gradually shift attention toward what is working, rather than only what feels hard. Over time, this kind of reflection may help build a more balanced sense of self.
- It can also be valuable to consider how others experience us. Sometimes the qualities we take for granted in ourselves are precisely the ones other people notice and appreciate most. Asking a trusted friend or family member what they value about you, or simply paying attention to positive feedback you often receive, can offer a perspective that is hard to access on our own.
- Another approach that some people find useful is exploring formal strengths assessments. Tools like character strengths frameworks, developed through positive psychology research, can offer a structured way to identify patterns in how we think, feel, and engage with the world. Becoming more aware of these patterns can inform how we approach challenges, relationships, and daily life.
- Recognizing your strengths is not about dismissing difficulties or forcing positivity. It is about building a fuller, more honest picture of yourself — one that includes what you bring to the world alongside what you find hard. If you are finding this difficult to do alone, speaking with a mental health professional can be a supportive place to explore these questions further.
