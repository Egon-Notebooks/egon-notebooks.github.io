- Knowing when and how to set limits at work is something many people find genuinely challenging. Whether it is a tendency to say yes to every request, difficulty logging off at the end of the day, or feeling responsible for other people's stress, the boundaries between our professional and personal lives can blur in ways that quietly wear us down over time. Recognising this pattern is an important first step.
- One strategy that many people find helpful is identifying what feels most pressing first. Rather than trying to change everything at once, choosing one specific area — such as not checking work messages after a certain hour — can make the process feel more manageable. Small, consistent changes often have a meaningful cumulative effect on wellbeing.
- Clear, calm communication can also make a real difference. Letting colleagues or managers know about your availability, or gently declining tasks that fall outside your agreed responsibilities, does not have to feel confrontational. Practising phrases in advance, or even writing them down, can help build confidence in moments that might otherwise feel uncomfortable. Over time, this kind of honest communication tends to strengthen working relationships rather than strain them.
- It is also worth paying attention to how work pressures show up in the body and mind. Persistent tiredness, difficulty concentrating, or a sense of dread at the start of the week are signals worth taking seriously. Many people find that noting these experiences — even briefly in a journal — helps them understand their own patterns and needs more clearly.
- If setting limits at work feels particularly difficult, or if stress has begun to affect daily life more broadly, talking to someone can help. Support is available, and reaching out — whether to a trusted person in your life or a mental health professional — is a sign of self-awareness, not weakness.
