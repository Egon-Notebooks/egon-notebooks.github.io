- Being there for someone who is being bullied can make a real difference in how they cope and recover. Bullying — whether it happens in person, online, or in the workplace — can affect a person's confidence, sense of safety, and overall wellbeing. Knowing how to offer meaningful support, rather than unintentionally minimizing the experience, is one of the most valuable things you can do.
- One of the most important things you can offer is simply being present and listening without judgment. Many people who are being bullied feel ashamed or worry they won't be believed, so creating a space where they feel safe to talk openly matters enormously. Avoid jumping straight to problem-solving or telling them what they should have done differently. Instead, acknowledge what they're going through and let them know you take it seriously.
- When the time feels right, gently encouraging the person to think about their options can be helpful. Many people find it useful to document incidents — keeping a record of what happened, when, and who was involved — especially if they decide to report the behavior to a school, employer, or another authority. Supporting them in exploring these steps at their own pace, rather than pushing them toward a particular course of action, helps preserve their sense of agency and control.
- It's also worth keeping an eye on their emotional wellbeing over time. Bullying can sometimes contribute to [[Understanding anxiety|anxiety]], [[Understanding low mood|low mood]], or withdrawal from activities and relationships a person once enjoyed. Gently checking in, staying connected, and encouraging them to speak with a mental health professional if they're struggling can all be meaningful forms of support.
- Nobody should have to navigate bullying alone, and recovery is possible. Being a consistent, caring presence in someone's life can be a genuinely powerful thing.
