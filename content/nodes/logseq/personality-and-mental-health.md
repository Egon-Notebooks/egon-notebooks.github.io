- Personality shapes the way we experience the world — how we relate to others, respond to stress, and find meaning in everyday life. It is a rich and complex part of who we are, and understanding its connection to mental health can be a genuinely empowering step toward greater self-awareness and wellbeing.
- Research suggests that certain personality traits can influence a person's vulnerability to, or resilience against, mental health challenges. For example, people who tend toward high levels of self-criticism or who find it difficult to tolerate uncertainty may be more likely to experience anxiety or low mood during difficult periods. This is not a fixed outcome — it simply means that understanding your own patterns can help you recognise when extra support might be useful.
- One area that many people find helpful is exploring the difference between enduring personality traits and temporary mental health states. Feeling persistently hopeless, for instance, may feel like "just who you are," but it can also be a sign that something is going on that is worth addressing with professional support. These two things — personality and mental health — can influence each other, but they are not the same, and what feels permanent often is not.
- Some strategies that may help include building self-awareness through journaling or mindful reflection, which can make it easier to notice patterns in how you think and feel. Engaging in therapy — particularly approaches such as cognitive behavioural therapy or schema therapy — can support people in understanding how long-held beliefs and ways of relating to the world affect their mental health. Talking with trusted people in your life can also offer perspective and a sense of connection.
- Nobody is defined by their personality in a limiting way, and mental health is something that can genuinely shift and improve. Reaching out for support is a meaningful step, and many people find that doing so opens up new possibilities they hadn't previously imagined.
