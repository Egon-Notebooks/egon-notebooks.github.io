- Feeling exhausted when you're going through a period of depression is one of the most common — and often least talked about — experiences. Depression can make even small, everyday tasks feel far more demanding than usual, and this isn't a sign of weakness or laziness. It's a recognized part of how depression affects the body and mind. Understanding this can be the first step toward being a little gentler with yourself.
- One approach many people find helpful is sometimes called "energy budgeting." Rather than pushing through a long to-do list and risking burnout, this involves identifying one or two small, meaningful tasks for the day and treating everything else as optional. Breaking tasks into smaller steps — for example, not "clean the kitchen" but "put three things away" — can make action feel more manageable and reduce the sense of overwhelm that often comes with low energy.
- Rest itself is worth reconsidering during difficult periods. Many people find that the quality of rest matters as much as the quantity. Short periods of deliberate, guilt-free rest — sitting quietly, lying down without screens, or spending time in a calming environment — can help the body recover in ways that distracted or anxious downtime often doesn't. Some people also notice that gentle movement, even a brief walk, can gradually shift their energy levels over time, though this will look different for everyone.
- It can also help to notice which activities tend to drain energy and which, even slightly, seem to restore it. This awareness, built slowly and without pressure, can support more intentional choices on harder days.
- If low energy and low mood have been affecting your daily life, you don't have to navigate this alone. Support is available, and many people find that with the right help, things can and do improve.
