- Fortune telling, in the context of mental health, refers to a common thinking pattern where a person assumes they already know how something will turn out — and usually expects the worst. This cognitive distortion, sometimes called "catastrophic prediction," can quietly fuel [[Understanding anxiety|anxiety]] and hold people back from trying new things, reconnecting with others, or imagining a different future for themselves.
- When someone experiences this pattern, thoughts like "I know it won't work out," "There's no point in trying," or "I can already tell they don't like me" can feel completely convincing — even when there is little or no evidence to support them. The mind presents these predictions as facts rather than possibilities, which can make it difficult to challenge them. Over time, acting on these assumptions can reinforce them, creating a cycle that is hard to break alone.
- One approach many people find helpful is learning to notice when fortune-telling thoughts arise and gently questioning them. Asking "What actual evidence do I have for this?" or "Have I been wrong about predictions like this before?" can create a small but meaningful pause between a thought and a reaction. Another strategy is to experiment with uncertainty — instead of avoiding a situation because the outcome feels inevitable, treating it as genuinely unknown and observing what actually happens. Keeping a simple journal of predictions versus real outcomes can also help reveal just how often our negative forecasts miss the mark.
- It is worth remembering that these thought patterns are not personal flaws — they are very common human experiences, often rooted in past hurts or prolonged [[Understanding stress|stress]]. With the right support, many people find that they are able to loosen the grip these predictions have on their daily lives. If fortune-telling thoughts are affecting your wellbeing, speaking with a mental health professional can be a meaningful step forward.
