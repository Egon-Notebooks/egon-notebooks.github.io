- Anxiety can sometimes make it feel as though the mind is racing far ahead of the present moment — caught up in worries about the future or replaying events from the past. Grounding techniques are simple, practical strategies that many people find helpful for gently bringing attention back to the here and now. They work by engaging the senses and the body, which can help interrupt the cycle of anxious thoughts.
- One widely used approach is the 5-4-3-2-1 technique. This involves slowly noticing five things you can see, four things you can physically feel (such as the texture of a surface or your feet on the floor), three things you can hear, two things you can smell, and one thing you can taste. Moving through each sense at a deliberate pace can help anchor awareness in the present environment, which many people find calming during moments of heightened anxiety.
- Slow, intentional breathing is another grounding strategy with a strong evidence base. Breathing in for a count of four, holding briefly, and breathing out for a count of six or longer can activate the body's natural calming response. This works because lengthening the exhale signals to the nervous system that it is safe to settle. Many people find this easier to practise regularly when they build it into an existing routine, such as before a meal or before sleep.
- Physical grounding — such as pressing your feet firmly into the floor, holding something cold or textured, or gently splashing cool water on your face — can also help shift focus away from anxious thoughts and back into the body.
- If anxiety is affecting daily life, connecting with a mental health professional can make a meaningful difference. Support is available, and with the right tools and guidance, many people find that anxiety becomes much more manageable over time.
