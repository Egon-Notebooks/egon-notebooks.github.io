- Many of us experience moments when our minds seem to work against us — replaying past mistakes, jumping to worst-case scenarios, or telling us we are not good enough. These patterns are sometimes called unhelpful or distorted thinking, and they are an incredibly common part of being human. The encouraging news is that thoughts are not facts, and with practice, it is possible to gently challenge the ones that hold us back.
- One approach that many people find useful is simply pausing to notice a thought before accepting it as true. When a difficult thought arises — such as "I always fail" or "nobody cares about me" — try asking yourself what evidence actually supports it, and what evidence might contradict it. This is not about forcing positivity, but about looking at the situation more completely and honestly.
- Another strategy involves considering what you might say to a friend who had the same thought. Most of us are far kinder to others than we are to ourselves. Bringing that same compassion inward can help soften the grip of harsh self-critical thinking. Over time, this kind of gentle questioning can begin to shift familiar thought patterns in a meaningful way.
- It can also help to recognize that having a thought does not mean you have to engage with it or believe it. Some people find it useful to acknowledge a thought — "I notice I am having the thought that..." — and then let it pass without getting pulled into it. This small shift in perspective can create a little breathing room between you and the thought.
- Learning to work with unhelpful thoughts takes time and patience, and it is not something anyone has to figure out alone. Speaking with a mental health professional can offer personalized guidance and support, and many people find that doing so makes a genuine difference.
