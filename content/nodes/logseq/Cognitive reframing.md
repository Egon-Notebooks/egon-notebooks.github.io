- Thoughts have a powerful influence on how we feel and how we move through daily life. When difficult emotions arise, it can be easy to get caught in patterns of thinking that feel fixed and absolute — but many people find that gently shifting the way they interpret a situation can make a meaningful difference to their emotional wellbeing. This approach is often called cognitive reframing.
- Cognitive reframing is a technique rooted in cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), a well-researched form of psychological support. At its core, it involves noticing an unhelpful thought and exploring whether there is another, more balanced way to look at the same situation. This isn't about forcing positivity or dismissing real pain — it's about creating a little more space between an experience and the story we tell ourselves about it.
- One way to practice this is to notice when a thought feels very all-or-nothing, such as "I always fail" or "nothing ever goes right." Many people find it helpful to gently ask themselves: is this entirely true? What evidence is there for and against this thought? What might I say to a friend who was thinking this way? These questions can soften rigid thinking and open up new perspectives.
- Another strategy involves looking for what a difficult experience might be teaching you. This isn't about minimizing hardship, but about recognizing that struggle can sometimes point toward values, [[Setting healthy boundaries|boundaries]], or needs that deserve attention. Journaling about a challenging situation from this angle is something many people find useful over time.
- Learning to reframe thoughts is a skill, and like any skill, it takes practice. It can also be easier to develop with the guidance of a trained professional. If you find that unhelpful thought patterns are significantly affecting your life, reaching out to a mental health professional is a meaningful and courageous step — and support is available.
