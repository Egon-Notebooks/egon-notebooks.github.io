- Perfectionism is something many people can relate to — that persistent feeling that what you do must be flawless, or that anything less than perfect simply isn't good enough. While having high standards can be a strength, perfectionism crosses a line when it begins to cause significant stress, self-criticism, or avoidance of things we care about.
- One strategy many people find helpful is learning to notice the difference between healthy striving and perfectionist thinking. Healthy striving means working toward meaningful goals while accepting that mistakes are part of the process. Perfectionism, by contrast, often ties a person's sense of worth entirely to their performance or output. Simply becoming aware of this pattern — without judgment — can be a meaningful first step.
- Another approach that some people find useful is practising self-compassion. This means treating yourself with the same kindness you might offer a friend who made a mistake or fell short of a goal. Research suggests that self-compassion can actually support motivation and resilience better than harsh self-criticism, which often leads to burnout or avoidance rather than genuine improvement.
- It can also help to experiment with intentionally doing things imperfectly in low-stakes situations. Some people find that finishing something "good enough" — rather than endlessly revising — gradually loosens perfectionism's grip and builds confidence that things don't have to be flawless to have value.
- Perfectionism is not a fixed personality trait. It is a pattern that developed for reasons that often made sense at the time, and it is a pattern that can shift. Many people find that working with a mental health professional helps them understand where their perfectionism comes from and develop a healthier relationship with themselves and their goals. Reaching out for support is not a sign of weakness — it is itself an act of courage.
