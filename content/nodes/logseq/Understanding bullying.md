- Bullying is an experience that can affect people of all ages, and its impact on mental health is real and significant. Whether it happens in person or online, bullying involves repeated behavior intended to hurt, exclude, or control someone — and understanding what it is and how it affects us is an important first step toward healing and prevention.
- One of the most important things to recognize is that being bullied is never the fault of the person experiencing it. Bullying can affect [[Self-esteem and where it comes from|self-esteem]], increase feelings of [[Understanding anxiety|anxiety]] or [[Sadness|sadness]], and make social situations feel unsafe. Many people who have been bullied describe a sense of isolation, but it can help to know that these feelings are understandable responses to a difficult situation — and they do not have to be permanent.
- There are several approaches that many people find helpful when navigating the effects of bullying. Talking to someone trusted — a friend, family member, teacher, or colleague — can reduce the sense of isolation that bullying often creates. Keeping a record of incidents may also help when reporting bullying to a school, workplace, or platform. For those experiencing longer-lasting emotional effects, working with a mental health professional can provide a space to process what happened and build [[Building resilience|resilience]] over time.
- It is also worth noting that people who engage in bullying behavior are often dealing with their own unmet needs or difficulties. This does not excuse harmful behavior, but understanding this can sometimes help reduce the weight of self-blame.
- If bullying has affected your mental health or the mental health of someone you care about, reaching out for support is a meaningful and courageous step. With the right help, it is possible to rebuild confidence, strengthen connections, and move forward.
