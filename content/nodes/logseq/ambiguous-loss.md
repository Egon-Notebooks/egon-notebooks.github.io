- Grief does not always come with a clear ending. Sometimes loss arrives without a death, a farewell, or a defined moment of goodbye — and this kind of grief can be some of the hardest to carry. Psychologist Pauline Boss gave this experience a name: ambiguous loss. It describes the pain of losing someone who is still physically present but emotionally or cognitively absent, such as a loved one living with dementia or addiction, as well as the grief felt when someone is physically gone but their fate remains unknown, such as in cases of disappearance or estrangement.
- One reason ambiguous loss can feel so isolating is that it often goes unrecognized by others. Without a clear event to point to, people may feel their grief is invisible or invalid. Acknowledging that this is a real and legitimate form of loss — to yourself and, where possible, to those around you — can be a meaningful first step. Many people find that simply naming what they are experiencing brings a small but important sense of relief.
- Finding ways to hold both the loss and the ongoing relationship at the same time is something many people find helpful. This might look like creating new rituals, finding community with others in similar situations, or allowing yourself to grieve what was while still tending to what remains. Some people also find it useful to work with a therapist who understands grief, as professional support can offer a space to process feelings that are difficult to sit with alone.
- Living with ambiguous loss takes real courage, and it is something many people navigate — often more quietly than the world realizes. With time, support, and self-compassion, it is possible to find a sense of meaning and steadiness, even when the loss itself remains unresolved.
