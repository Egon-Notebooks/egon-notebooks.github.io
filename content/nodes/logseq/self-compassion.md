- Self-compassion is the practice of treating yourself with the same kindness and understanding you would offer a close friend who is struggling. For many people, it can feel unfamiliar or even uncomfortable — particularly for those who have grown up believing that self-criticism is what keeps them motivated or accountable. In reality, research consistently suggests that self-compassion supports emotional resilience and is linked to greater wellbeing over time.
- One of the most practical ways to begin building self-compassion is to notice your inner voice. When something goes wrong or you make a mistake, pay attention to how you speak to yourself. Many people find it helpful to pause and ask: "Would I say this to someone I care about?" If the answer is no, that can be a gentle signal to try reframing the thought in a warmer, more understanding way — not to dismiss the difficulty, but to hold it with more care.
- Another approach that some people find useful is the practice of acknowledging shared humanity. Difficult emotions, setbacks, and feelings of inadequacy are part of being human — not signs of personal failure. Recognising that others experience similar struggles can reduce the sense of isolation that often makes hard times feel even harder. Mindfulness practices, such as simply observing difficult feelings without judgment, may also support this process by creating a little space between an experience and your reaction to it.
- It is worth knowing that self-compassion is not something you either have or don't — it is a skill that can be learned and strengthened gradually. Many people find that working with a mental health professional can help them explore self-compassion in a way that feels grounded and personal to their own experiences. Support is available, and small steps in this direction can make a meaningful difference.
