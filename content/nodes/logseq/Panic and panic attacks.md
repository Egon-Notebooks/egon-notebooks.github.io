- Panic attacks can be an overwhelming and frightening experience. The sudden surge of intense [[Fear|fear]] — often accompanied by a racing heart, shortness of breath, dizziness, or a sense that something is terribly wrong — can feel completely out of the blue and hard to make sense of. Understanding what panic attacks are and how they work can be an important first step toward feeling less afraid of them.
- One thing many people find reassuring is learning that panic attacks, while deeply uncomfortable, are not physically dangerous. The body's alarm system is essentially misfiring — triggering a "fight or flight" response when there is no actual threat present. Recognizing this in the moment can be difficult, but over time, some people find it helpful to gently remind themselves: "This feeling is intense, but it will pass." Panic attacks typically peak within a few minutes and gradually subside on their own.
- Slow, controlled breathing is one of the most widely supported strategies for managing panic in the moment. Breathing out for longer than you breathe in — for example, inhaling for four counts and exhaling for six — can help signal to the nervous system that the body is safe. Grounding techniques, such as noticing five things you can see or feel in your immediate surroundings, are another approach that many people find useful for reconnecting with the present moment.
- For those who experience panic attacks frequently, it is worth knowing that effective support is available. Therapies such as cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) — a structured approach that helps people understand and gently shift unhelpful thought patterns — have a strong evidence base for treating panic. Speaking with a doctor or mental health professional can open the door to personalized care and a clearer path forward. Experiencing panic does not have to be a permanent part of life, and many people find meaningful relief with the right support.
