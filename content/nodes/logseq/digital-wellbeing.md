- Screens are woven into nearly every part of modern life — how we work, connect, rest, and learn. This makes it genuinely difficult to know when our relationship with technology is supporting us and when it might be quietly wearing us down. Digital wellbeing is about finding a balance that works for you, and small, intentional shifts can make a meaningful difference.
- One area many people find worth exploring is how technology affects sleep. Screens emit blue light that can interfere with the body's natural wind-down process, and the mental stimulation of scrolling or notifications can make it harder to settle at night. Many people find that stepping away from devices for 30 to 60 minutes before bed — even occasionally — helps their sleep feel more restful over time.
- Another helpful practice is becoming more aware of how different types of digital activity leave you feeling. Connecting with people you care about online, learning something new, or being creative can feel genuinely energizing. Passive, aimless scrolling, on the other hand, can sometimes leave people feeling flat or restless. Noticing these patterns — without judgment — can help you make more conscious choices about how you spend your time online.
- Setting gentle boundaries can also support wellbeing. This might look like turning off non-essential notifications, designating certain times of day as screen-free, or keeping phones out of certain spaces like the dinner table or bedroom. These are not rules to follow rigidly, but possibilities to experiment with.
- If you find that your digital habits feel difficult to change, or that technology use is affecting your mood, relationships, or daily functioning in ways that concern you, it can be worth speaking with a mental health professional. Support is available, and it is entirely possible to develop a healthier, more intentional relationship with the digital world.
