- Mind reading — the habit of assuming we know what other people are thinking — is one of the most common thinking patterns that can quietly affect our emotional wellbeing. Most of us have experienced that sinking feeling of being certain a friend is annoyed with us, or that a colleague thinks poorly of our work, even when nothing has actually been said. While this tendency is a normal part of being human, it can become a source of significant [[Understanding stress|stress]] and [[Understanding anxiety|anxiety]] when it goes unchecked.
- One of the most helpful things a person can do is simply notice when mind reading is happening. Pausing to ask "Do I actually know this, or am I guessing?" can create a small but meaningful gap between a thought and an emotional reaction. Many people find that writing these thoughts down helps them see how often assumptions fill in the spaces where real information is missing.
- It can also help to gently test assumptions rather than treating them as facts. This might mean checking in with someone directly — asking a clarifying question rather than acting on an interpretation. These small conversations can feel uncomfortable at first, but many people find they ease anxiety and strengthen relationships over time. [[Cognitive behavioral therapy]] (CBT), a well-researched approach to mental health, identifies mind reading as a type of "cognitive distortion" — a thinking shortcut that the brain uses but that doesn't always reflect reality. Working with a therapist can help someone explore these patterns in a supported, structured way.
- It's worth remembering that minds are genuinely hard to read, even for the people closest to us. Noticing this pattern doesn't mean something is wrong — it means a person is becoming more aware of how their thinking affects their feelings. With the right support and practice, many people find they can relate to their thoughts with greater curiosity and a little less certainty.
