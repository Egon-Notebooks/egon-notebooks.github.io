- All-or-nothing thinking — sometimes called black-and-white thinking — is a common pattern where situations, people, or even ourselves are seen in absolute terms, with no middle ground. Something is either a total success or a complete failure. A person is either entirely trustworthy or not at all. While this kind of thinking can feel like clarity, it often creates unnecessary distress and can make challenges feel much larger than they are.
- One way to work with this pattern is to practice noticing when absolute language appears in your thoughts. Words like "always," "never," "everyone," or "no one" can be signals that all-or-nothing thinking may be at play. Simply pausing to ask "Is this really always true?" or "Are there any exceptions?" can gently open up space between the extremes. Many people find that even identifying a single exception helps loosen the hold of an absolute belief.
- Another approach involves deliberately exploring the middle ground. Rather than rating an experience as a success or failure, some people find it helpful to think in percentages or along a spectrum — asking, "What went reasonably well? What could I improve next time?" This kind of thinking, sometimes called cognitive flexibility, tends to feel more realistic and is associated with greater emotional resilience over time.
- It can also help to treat yourself with the same compassion you might offer a friend. All-or-nothing thinking is often harshest when turned inward, making small setbacks feel like proof of fundamental failure. Recognizing that imperfection is a universal human experience — not a personal flaw — can gradually shift this perspective.
- If you notice these patterns are significantly affecting your wellbeing or daily life, speaking with a mental health professional can make a real difference. Support is available, and with the right tools, thinking patterns can change.
