- Narrative therapy is a collaborative approach to mental health support that invites people to think about their lives as stories — ones that can be explored, reframed, and retold. Developed in the 1980s by therapists Michael White and David Epston, it is built on the idea that we are not defined by our struggles. Instead, the difficulties we face are seen as separate from who we are as people.
- One of the core ideas in narrative therapy is called "externalizing the problem." Rather than thinking "I am anxious," a person might explore "[[Understanding anxiety|anxiety]] is something that shows up in my life." This small but meaningful shift can create some psychological distance between a person and a difficult experience, making it feel less overwhelming and more possible to work with. Many people find that this perspective opens up new ways of thinking about challenges they had previously felt stuck inside.
- Another key element involves identifying "unique outcomes" — moments when the problem did not have as much influence, or when a person acted in ways that felt true to their values. Reflecting on these moments can help build a fuller, more balanced sense of self. Over time, a therapist and client might work together to develop what is called a "preferred story" — a richer account of who the person is that goes beyond the difficulties they have been through.
- Narrative therapy is typically practiced with a trained therapist, though some people find that reflective journaling or talking with trusted others can offer a taste of its principles in everyday life.
- If you have been feeling trapped by a particular story about yourself or your life, support is available. Speaking with a mental health professional can offer a safe space to explore new perspectives — and many people find that change, even when it feels far away, is more possible than they expected.
