- Self-esteem is often described as the overall sense of value or worth we hold about ourselves. It shapes how we interpret our experiences, how we relate to others, and how we approach challenges in daily life. Understanding where self-esteem comes from can be a meaningful first step toward nurturing a healthier relationship with yourself.
- Much of our self-esteem develops early in life, shaped by the messages we receive from caregivers, peers, and the wider world around us. Repeated experiences of feeling heard, valued, and encouraged tend to support a more stable sense of self-worth, while experiences of criticism, neglect, or comparison can leave lasting impressions. Importantly, these early influences are not the final word — the brain remains capable of change, and self-esteem can shift and grow throughout adulthood.
- One way many people find helpful in building self-esteem is paying gentle attention to self-talk — the internal voice that narrates our experiences. When that voice is consistently harsh or dismissive, it can quietly erode confidence over time. Some people find that noticing unhelpful thoughts, rather than automatically believing them, creates a little space for a kinder perspective to emerge. Another approach that research supports is engaging in activities that align with personal values, as this can reinforce a sense of competence and purpose that goes beyond external approval.
- It is also worth recognizing that self-esteem does not have to mean feeling confident all the time. Fluctuations are normal, and struggling with self-worth is something many people experience at different points in their lives — it is not a reflection of weakness or permanent character.
- If low self-esteem is affecting your day-to-day life, speaking with a mental health professional can offer valuable support and guidance. Reaching out is a sign of self-awareness, and things can genuinely improve with the right help.
