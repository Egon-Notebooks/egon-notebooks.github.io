- When someone we care about is grieving, it can be hard to know what to say or do. The desire to help is often strong, but many people worry about saying the wrong thing or making the situation more painful. Understanding a little about grief — and how to show up for someone experiencing it — can make a real difference.
- One of the most meaningful things you can do is simply be present. Grief can be an isolating experience, and knowing that someone is there — whether that means sitting quietly together, checking in with a message, or offering practical help like preparing a meal — can provide real comfort. Many people find that they do not need answers or advice; they need to feel that they are not alone.
- It also helps to follow the grieving person's lead. Grief does not follow a predictable path, and people move through it in their own time and in their own way. Some days a person may want to talk about the one they have lost; other days they may prefer distraction or silence. Asking gently what kind of support feels helpful — rather than assuming — shows respect for their experience and their needs.
- It is also worth remembering that grief does not end after the funeral or the first few weeks. Reaching out over the longer term, including on anniversaries or other significant dates, can mean a great deal to someone who may feel that the world has moved on while they are still carrying their loss.
- Supporting a grieving person can be emotionally demanding, and it is natural to feel uncertain or even helpless at times. If you are concerned about someone's wellbeing, or if they seem to be struggling significantly, encouraging them to speak with a professional can be a gentle and caring step. Grief is a deeply human experience — and no one has to face it without support.
