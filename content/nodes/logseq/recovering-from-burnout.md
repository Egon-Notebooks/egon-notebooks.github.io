- Burnout can creep up gradually — a persistent exhaustion that goes beyond ordinary tiredness, often accompanied by a sense of detachment, reduced motivation, and feeling like even small tasks take enormous effort. It is a recognised response to prolonged stress, and it is far more common than many people realise. Importantly, burnout is not a personal failing, and recovery is possible.
- One of the most meaningful steps in recovering from burnout is addressing the conditions that contributed to it. This might mean having honest conversations about workload, setting clearer boundaries around time and energy, or identifying which responsibilities might be adjusted or shared. This is not always straightforward, but even small changes to daily demands can create space for the nervous system to begin settling.
- Rest is a central part of recovery, though many people find that genuine rest feels surprisingly difficult at first. Rest is not only sleep — it also includes activities that feel restorative rather than productive, such as spending time in nature, engaging in creative outlets, or simply allowing unstructured time without pressure to achieve. Many people find that gradually reintroducing enjoyable activities — rather than forcing them — helps restore a sense of connection to life outside of obligations.
- Self-compassion also plays an important role. People recovering from burnout often carry guilt about what they feel unable to do. Practising a kinder inner dialogue, and acknowledging that recovery takes time, can make the process feel less overwhelming.
- Burnout can feel isolating, but it is a deeply human experience and one that many people move through and beyond. Talking with a trusted person in your life, or reaching out to a mental health professional, can provide meaningful support during recovery. Asking for help is not a sign of weakness — it is often one of the most effective steps a person can take.
