- Focusing on personal strengths can feel unfamiliar, especially during difficult times when challenges tend to feel louder than achievements. Yet recognizing what we do well — and the qualities that help us navigate life — is a meaningful part of supporting overall mental wellbeing.
- One place to start is by reflecting on past experiences. Many people find it helpful to think about moments when they managed something hard, supported someone else, or worked through a problem. The qualities that showed up in those moments — patience, creativity, persistence, humor — are real strengths, even if they don't always feel like it. Writing these reflections down in a journal can make them easier to recognize and return to over time.
- It can also help to pay attention to feedback from people you trust. Sometimes others notice strengths in us before we notice them in ourselves. A trusted friend, family member, or colleague might reflect back qualities that feel ordinary to you but are genuinely valued by those around you. Receiving that kind of observation with openness, rather than brushing it off, is itself a small but meaningful practice.
- Another approach is to notice what feels energizing. Strengths aren't only about what we're good at — they're also connected to what feels meaningful and engaging. Activities or interactions that leave a person feeling more alive, curious, or purposeful often point toward an underlying strength worth acknowledging.
- Recognizing your strengths doesn't mean ignoring areas where you'd like to grow. It means building a more complete and honest picture of who you are. For many people, this kind of self-awareness becomes a foundation for [[Building resilience|resilience]]. If you're finding it difficult to see your own strengths, speaking with a mental health professional can offer valuable guidance and perspective — support is available, and it can make a real difference.
