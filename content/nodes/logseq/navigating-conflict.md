- Disagreements and tension are a natural part of being in relationship with others — whether with a partner, family member, friend, or colleague. Conflict itself is not a sign that something is wrong; it is simply what happens when two people with different needs, experiences, or perspectives occupy the same space. What matters most is not whether conflict arises, but how we move through it.
- One approach that many people find helpful is pausing before responding. When emotions are running high, the body's stress response can make it harder to think clearly or listen well. Taking a few slow breaths, or even stepping away briefly with an agreement to return to the conversation, can create enough space to respond rather than react. This small shift can change the tone of an entire exchange.
- Another strategy involves focusing on your own experience rather than the other person's behaviour. Expressing how a situation has affected you — rather than leading with blame or criticism — tends to invite more openness. Phrases like "I felt hurt when..." can open a conversation that "you always..." tends to close. Alongside this, genuinely listening to understand, rather than to prepare a counter-argument, can help both people feel heard, which is often what is needed most.
- It is also worth recognising when a conflict feels too big or too entrenched to resolve alone. Patterns of ongoing conflict, or situations where the relationship feels unsafe, are signs that additional support may be valuable. Therapists and counsellors who specialise in communication and relationships can offer tools and a neutral space to work things through.
- Conflict is not something to be feared or avoided at all costs. With care and practice, it can become an opportunity for deeper understanding — and many people find that navigating it well strengthens their relationships over time.
