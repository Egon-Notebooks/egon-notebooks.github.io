- Thoughts are powerful — but they are not always accurate. Most people, at some point, experience patterns of thinking that feel completely true in the moment but that can quietly fuel stress, low mood, or anxiety. These patterns are often called cognitive distortions, which simply means habitual ways of thinking that tend to skew how we see ourselves, other people, or the world around us.
- One of the most common examples is "all-or-nothing thinking," where situations feel either completely perfect or entirely ruined, with no middle ground. A small mistake might feel like total failure. Another frequent pattern is "catastrophising," which involves imagining that the worst possible outcome is the most likely one — turning a difficult conversation at work, for instance, into a certainty that everything is about to fall apart. A third pattern, sometimes called "mind reading," happens when we assume we know what others are thinking, usually something negative, without any real evidence to support it.
- Recognising these patterns is a meaningful first step. Many people find it helpful to gently pause when a thought feels overwhelming and ask: "Is there another way to look at this?" or "What evidence do I actually have for this thought?" This approach, rooted in cognitive behavioural therapy (a widely researched form of talking therapy), is not about forcing positivity — it is about making space for a more balanced perspective. Keeping a simple journal to notice recurring thoughts can also help some people identify patterns over time.
- It is worth remembering that experiencing distorted thinking does not mean something is fundamentally wrong with you — it means you are human. These patterns can shift with awareness and support. If unhelpful thinking is affecting your daily life, speaking with a mental health professional can make a real difference. Help is available, and change is possible.
