- Dialectical behavior therapy, often referred to as DBT, is a structured, evidence-based approach to therapy that helps people build skills for managing intense emotions, navigating relationships, and coping with distress. Originally developed to support people experiencing severe emotional dysregulation, DBT is now used across a wide range of mental health challenges and is recognized for its practical, skill-focused approach.
- One of the core ideas in DBT is the concept of dialectics — the practice of holding two seemingly opposite things as true at the same time. A central example of this is the balance between acceptance and change: acknowledging that you are doing the best you can, while also recognizing that growth and new skills are possible. Many people find this framing reduces self-judgment and creates a more compassionate foundation for personal work.
- DBT is typically organized around four skill areas. Mindfulness helps people become more aware of their thoughts and feelings without immediately reacting to them. Distress tolerance offers strategies for getting through difficult moments without making situations worse. Emotion regulation focuses on understanding and gently shifting emotional responses over time. Interpersonal effectiveness builds communication and boundary-setting skills that can strengthen relationships and reduce conflict. These skill areas are designed to work together, and many people find that practicing even one of them can have a meaningful effect on daily life.
- DBT is often delivered through a combination of individual therapy sessions and group skills training, though approaches can vary depending on the provider and setting. It is a collaborative process — therapists and clients work as a team, with the goal of building a life that feels more manageable and meaningful.
- If you are curious about whether DBT might be a helpful fit for you, speaking with a mental health professional is a good place to start. Support is available, and change is possible.
