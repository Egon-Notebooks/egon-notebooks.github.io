- Anxiety can show up in many ways — a racing heart, shallow breaths, a sense of dread that is hard to shake. When this happens, the body's stress response has been activated, and one of the most accessible ways to gently work with it is through intentional breathing. Simple breathing exercises won't eliminate anxiety entirely, but many people find they can help create a small moment of calm in the middle of a difficult experience.
- One approach that many people find helpful is slow, extended exhaling. When we are anxious, we tend to breathe quickly and shallowly. Deliberately slowing the breath — and making the exhale longer than the inhale — can signal to the nervous system that it is safe to settle. A simple version of this involves breathing in for a count of four, then breathing out for a count of six or eight. Even a few cycles of this can begin to shift how the body feels.
- Another technique is sometimes called box breathing. This involves inhaling for four counts, holding gently for four counts, exhaling for four counts, and pausing for four counts before the next breath. This structured rhythm gives the mind something steady to focus on, which can interrupt the spiral of anxious thoughts. Many people find this particularly useful before a stressful situation, such as a difficult conversation or a medical appointment.
- It is worth knowing that breathing exercises take a little practice. They may feel awkward at first, and that is completely normal. Some people find that practising during calmer moments helps these techniques feel more natural when anxiety is higher.
- Anxiety is something many people live with, and support is available. Speaking with a healthcare professional or mental health practitioner can open the door to a wider range of tools and approaches tailored to your individual needs.
