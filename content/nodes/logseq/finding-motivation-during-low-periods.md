- Finding motivation during low periods can feel like searching for something in the dark — you know it should be there, but reaching it seems impossibly hard. This experience is very common, and it does not mean something is permanently wrong. Many people go through phases where energy, interest, and drive feel distant, and there are ways to gently work toward reconnecting with them.
- One approach that many people find helpful is reducing the size of what they expect from themselves. During low periods, the gap between where we are and where we think we should be can feel overwhelming. Breaking tasks into very small, concrete steps — something as simple as making a cup of tea or stepping outside for a few minutes — can create a sense of forward movement without adding pressure. These small actions can gradually build momentum over time.
- Another strategy worth exploring is paying attention to the conditions that make motivation slightly more accessible. For some people, this might mean moving their body in a gentle way, spending time in natural light, or connecting briefly with someone they trust. Research suggests that mood and motivation are closely linked to physical factors like sleep, movement, and social connection, even when those things feel hard to prioritize.
- It can also help to let go of the idea that motivation needs to come before action. Many people find that a small amount of action — however modest — actually helps generate motivation, rather than the other way around. Starting before feeling ready is a skill that tends to develop with practice.
- If low motivation has been persistent or is significantly affecting daily life, reaching out to a mental health professional can make a real difference. Support is available, and with the right help, things can shift. You do not have to navigate this alone.
