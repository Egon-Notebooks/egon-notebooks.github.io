- Mindfulness is the practice of paying attention to the present moment — thoughts, feelings, and physical sensations — with openness and without judgment. It sounds simple, but for many people it can feel like a significant shift from the habit of replaying the past or worrying about the future. The good news is that mindfulness is a skill, and like any skill, it can be developed gradually over time.
- One of the most accessible ways to begin practicing mindfulness is through focused breathing. This involves pausing for a few minutes and gently directing attention to the rhythm of each breath. When the mind wanders — and it will — the practice is simply to notice that it has wandered and bring attention back, without self-criticism. Many people find that even a few minutes of this kind of intentional focus can create a small but meaningful sense of calm.
- Mindfulness can also be woven into everyday activities. Eating, walking, or even washing dishes can become an opportunity to practice by slowing down and noticing sensory details — textures, sounds, temperature — rather than moving through tasks on autopilot. Research suggests that this kind of present-moment awareness may help reduce [[Understanding stress|stress]], support emotional regulation, and improve overall well-being over time.
- A third approach that some people find helpful is a body scan, which involves mentally checking in with different parts of the body from head to toe, noticing any tension or sensation without trying to change it. This can be a gentle way to reconnect with physical experience, especially during periods of heightened [[Understanding stress|stress]] or [[Understanding anxiety|anxiety]].
- Mindfulness is not about achieving a perfectly quiet mind or feeling a certain way. It is simply about showing up to your experience with a little more curiosity and kindness. If you are interested in exploring mindfulness further, a mental health professional can help guide you in finding an approach that fits your life.
