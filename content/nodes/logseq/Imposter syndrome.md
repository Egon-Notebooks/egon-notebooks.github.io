- Feeling like a fraud despite evidence of your own competence is something many people experience — and it has a name. Imposter syndrome refers to an internal pattern of doubting your achievements and living with a quiet [[Fear|fear]] that others will eventually "find out" you don't belong where you are. It is remarkably common, showing up across workplaces, academic settings, and creative fields, and it can affect people at every level of experience and success.
- One strategy that many people find helpful is keeping a record of their accomplishments. This doesn't need to be elaborate — even a simple note on your phone where you save positive feedback, completed goals, or moments you felt proud can serve as a grounding reminder when self-doubt creeps in. Over time, this kind of evidence log can gently challenge the inner voice that insists your success was luck or a mistake.
- It can also help to talk openly about these feelings, whether with a trusted colleague, a friend, or a therapist. Many people are surprised to discover that others they admire experience the same doubts. Naming the feeling — "this is imposter syndrome" — can create a small but meaningful distance between you and the thought, making it easier to observe without being overwhelmed by it.
- Reframing the way you think about mistakes and learning is another approach worth exploring. Many people who experience imposter syndrome hold themselves to very high standards. Recognizing that uncertainty and growth go hand in hand — and that not knowing everything is a normal part of developing any skill — can gradually ease the pressure to appear flawless.
- If imposter syndrome is affecting your wellbeing, your relationships, or your ability to engage with work and life, support is available. Speaking with a mental health professional can offer a compassionate space to explore these patterns and develop tools that work for you. You don't have to navigate it alone.
