- Feeling at ease in your own body is something many people find genuinely challenging. Body image — the way we perceive and feel about our physical appearance — is shaped by a complex mix of personal experiences, relationships, cultural messages, and the media we consume. When body image becomes a source of persistent distress, it can quietly affect self-esteem, relationships, and overall wellbeing. The good news is that body image is not fixed, and many people find that their relationship with their body can shift over time with intention and support.
- One approach that many people find helpful is practicing mindful awareness of critical self-talk. Negative thoughts about our bodies can become so automatic that we barely notice them. Gently observing these thoughts — rather than accepting them as facts — can create a little space between the thought and how we feel. Over time, some people find it useful to consciously redirect those thoughts toward what their body can do, rather than how it looks.
- Another evidence-informed strategy involves curating the environment around you. This might mean reflecting on how certain media, social feeds, or conversations affect how you feel about yourself, and gradually making choices that bring in more diverse, affirming representations of bodies. Surrounding yourself, even digitally, with content that promotes acceptance rather than comparison can make a meaningful difference for many people.
- It also helps to nurture self-compassion — treating yourself with the same kindness you would offer a close friend. Research suggests that self-compassion, rather than self-criticism, is more likely to support lasting positive change in how we feel about ourselves.
- Struggling with body image is a widely shared human experience, and it is not something anyone has to work through alone. Speaking with a mental health professional can offer a supportive space to explore these feelings more deeply and develop a kinder relationship with yourself.
