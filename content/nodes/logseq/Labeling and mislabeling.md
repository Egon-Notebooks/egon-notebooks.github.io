- Labeling — the way we name and interpret our own experiences, traits, and behaviors — is something most people do without thinking. It's a natural part of how the mind tries to make sense of a complex world. But the labels we apply to ourselves can have a quiet and powerful effect on how we feel, how we act, and what we believe is possible for us.
- When labeling becomes mislabeling, it often shows up as overly harsh or [[All-or-nothing thinking|all-or-nothing thinking]]. For example, making a mistake at work and concluding "I am a failure" turns a single event into a fixed identity. This kind of thinking pattern is recognized in [[Cognitive behavioral therapy|cognitive behavioral therapy]] (CBT) — a well-researched approach to mental health — as a cognitive distortion, meaning a thought pattern that feels true but doesn't accurately reflect reality. Mislabeling can quietly reinforce feelings of [[Shame and guilt|shame]], hopelessness, or [[Understanding anxiety|anxiety]] over time.
- One strategy many people find helpful is learning to separate actions from identity. Rather than "I am lazy," trying "I've been struggling to find motivation lately" keeps the door open for change. Another approach is to notice when a label feels absolute — words like "always," "never," or "completely" are often signals that a thought may be worth gently questioning. Some people find that writing these thoughts down and then challenging them, or sharing them with a trusted person, helps create a little distance from a negative label and see it more clearly.
- It's worth remembering that the way we talk to ourselves is something that can shift with practice and support. If self-critical or limiting labels feel persistent or overwhelming, speaking with a mental health professional can make a meaningful difference. Many people find that with the right support, they're able to develop a kinder and more accurate way of understanding themselves.
