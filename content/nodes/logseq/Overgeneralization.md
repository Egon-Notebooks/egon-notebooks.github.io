- Overgeneralization is a common thinking pattern that can quietly shape the way we see ourselves and the world around us. It happens when we take one difficult experience and draw a sweeping conclusion from it — for example, making a single mistake at work and deciding "I always mess everything up." While this kind of thinking is very human, when it becomes a habit it can contribute to feelings of hopelessness, low self-worth, and [[Understanding anxiety|anxiety]].
- One of the most helpful first steps is simply learning to notice the pattern. Words like "always," "never," "everyone," and "no one" are often signals that overgeneralization may be at work. When these words show up in a thought, it can be useful to pause and ask: is this actually true every single time, or does it just feel that way right now? Many people find that gently questioning the thought — rather than fighting it — creates a little breathing room between the feeling and the conclusion.
- Another strategy that many people find helpful is looking for evidence that challenges the overgeneralized thought. If the thought is "I never do anything right," it can help to think of even one small thing that went well recently. This isn't about forcing positivity — it's about building a more balanced, realistic picture. Over time, practices like journaling or talking things through with someone trusted can support this process.
- It is also worth knowing that overgeneralization is a pattern that can change. [[Cognitive behavioral therapy]], or CBT, is one well-researched approach that helps people identify and gently reshape these kinds of thought patterns. Working with a mental health professional can make a real difference, and reaching out for that support is a meaningful and courageous step.
