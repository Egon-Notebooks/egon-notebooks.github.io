- Workplace bullying is more common than many people realize, and its effects can reach well beyond the office. Repeated mistreatment at work — whether through public humiliation, exclusion, constant criticism, or having your contributions ignored — can take a serious toll on a person's emotional wellbeing, sleep, confidence, and sense of self. Recognizing what is happening is an important first step, and it is worth knowing that support is available.
- One strategy many people find helpful is documenting incidents as they occur. Keeping a private record of dates, what was said or done, and who was present can help clarify patterns over time and may be useful if a formal complaint becomes necessary. Writing things down can also help a person feel a greater sense of clarity and control in a situation that can otherwise feel overwhelming.
- Building a support network — both inside and outside of work — can also make a meaningful difference. Trusted colleagues, friends, or family members can offer perspective and remind a person that the treatment they are experiencing is not a reflection of their worth. Many people find that talking through their experiences helps to reduce the sense of isolation that workplace bullying often creates.
- It is also worth considering whether there are formal channels available, such as an HR department, a union representative, or an employee assistance program. These resources exist to help, and using them is a sign of strength, not weakness. For some people, speaking with a mental health professional can be a valuable part of working through the emotional impact of a difficult work environment.
- No one should feel they have to simply endure mistreatment at work. The effects of workplace bullying are real and valid, and with the right support, things can genuinely improve.
