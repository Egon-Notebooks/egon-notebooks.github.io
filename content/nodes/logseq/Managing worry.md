- Worry is something almost everyone experiences — those recurring thoughts about what might go wrong, what's uncertain, or what feels out of our control. While some degree of worry is a natural part of being human, it can sometimes grow louder and more persistent, making it harder to focus, rest, or feel at ease in daily life. The good news is that there are practical ways to work with worry rather than feeling overwhelmed by it.
- One approach that many people find helpful is setting aside a specific, limited time each day — often called "worry time" — to acknowledge and sit with their concerns. Rather than trying to push worries away throughout the day, this technique involves gently redirecting worry thoughts to a designated window of time. This can help prevent worry from spreading across the entire day and may give a greater sense of structure and control.
- Another strategy involves noticing the difference between worries that are actionable and those that are not. For concerns where a concrete step is possible — like making an appointment or having a conversation — taking even a small action can ease the sense of helplessness that worry often brings. For worries about things beyond our control, practices like mindful breathing or grounding exercises may help the nervous system settle, even when the situation itself hasn't changed.
- Journaling is also something many people find useful — writing out worries can help externalize them, making them feel less tangled and more manageable.
- It's worth remembering that persistent or intense worry doesn't have to be faced alone. Talking with a trusted person in your life can make a real difference, and mental health professionals are trained to help people understand and work through worry in meaningful ways. Reaching out is a sign of self-awareness and strength, and support is available.
