- Burnout is a state of deep exhaustion that can develop when prolonged stress — often related to work, caregiving, or other demanding responsibilities — goes unaddressed for too long. It is more than just feeling tired after a busy week. Burnout tends to build gradually, and many people don't recognize it until they are already significantly depleted. Understanding the signs can be an important first step toward getting things back on track.
- One of the most common signs of burnout is a persistent sense of emotional and physical exhaustion that doesn't improve with rest. A person experiencing burnout may find it difficult to feel motivated or engaged, even in activities they once enjoyed. This can sometimes be confused with depression, and in some cases the two can occur together — which is why paying attention to how long these feelings have been present, and how much they are affecting daily life, can be helpful information to share with a healthcare professional.
- Another sign to be aware of is a growing sense of detachment or cynicism — feeling disconnected from work, relationships, or a sense of purpose. Alongside this, many people notice a decline in their ability to concentrate, make decisions, or feel effective in their roles. Some people experiencing burnout also notice physical symptoms such as frequent headaches, disrupted sleep, or a lowered immune system, as chronic stress can affect the body as well as the mind.
- Many people find that naming what they are experiencing is itself a relief — a recognition that their struggle has a shape, and that it can change. Burnout is not a personal failing, and it does not have to be a permanent state. With the right support, whether through conversations with a trusted person, adjustments to workload, or professional guidance, recovery is genuinely possible.
