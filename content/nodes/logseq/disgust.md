- Disgust is one of our most powerful emotions — and one of the least talked about in conversations around mental health. While we often focus on sadness, anxiety, or anger, disgust plays a significant role in how we relate to ourselves, others, and the world around us. Understanding this emotion more clearly can be an important step toward emotional wellbeing.
- One of the ways disgust shows up in mental health is through self-directed feelings — sometimes called self-disgust or shame. Many people experiencing conditions such as depression, eating difficulties, or trauma find that disgust turns inward, affecting how they see their own body, thoughts, or past actions. Recognizing that this feeling is a common emotional response — and not an accurate reflection of a person's worth — can be a gentle first step in loosening its grip. Some people find it helpful to notice self-disgust with curiosity rather than judgment, as though observing a wave rather than being swept away by it.
- Building emotional literacy around disgust can also be valuable. Learning to name the emotion — "I'm noticing feelings of disgust right now" — can create a small but meaningful distance between a person and the intensity of what they're feeling. This kind of mindful awareness, often explored in therapies such as Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) or Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT), may help people respond to difficult emotions more thoughtfully rather than reacting automatically.
- It is also worth knowing that disgust sensitivity varies between people and can change over time. What feels overwhelming today is not necessarily fixed. Many people find that with the right support, their relationship with difficult emotions — including disgust — shifts in meaningful ways.
- If you find that feelings of disgust, toward yourself or others, are affecting your daily life, reaching out to a mental health professional can be a meaningful and hopeful step forward.
