- Disgust is one of the most powerful — and least talked about — human emotions. While feelings like [[Sadness|sadness]] or [[Understanding anxiety|anxiety]] tend to get more attention in mental health conversations, disgust plays a significant role in how people relate to themselves, others, and the world around them. Understanding this emotion more clearly can be an important step toward emotional well-being.
- Disgust evolved as a protective response, originally helping humans avoid things that posed a physical threat, like spoiled food or disease. Over time, though, the emotion became more complex. Today, people can experience disgust in social situations, in response to moral violations, or — perhaps most painfully — directed inward at themselves. This last form, sometimes called self-disgust, is particularly worth paying attention to, as it is connected to feelings of [[Shame and guilt|shame]] and has been linked to difficulties with mood, [[Self-esteem and where it comes from|self-esteem]], and certain mental health conditions.
- One strategy many people find helpful is developing awareness of when disgust arises and what it may be responding to. Like all emotions, disgust carries information — but that information isn't always accurate or proportionate. Practicing a moment of gentle curiosity, rather than immediately reacting, can create useful space between the feeling and the response. Some people also find that compassion-focused approaches, including [[Self-compassion|self-compassion]] exercises, help soften the self-directed form of disgust over time. Working with a trained therapist can be especially valuable here, as patterns of self-disgust can run deep and are often connected to earlier experiences.
- It is worth remembering that experiencing intense or uncomfortable emotions — including disgust — does not say anything fixed about who a person is. Emotions are temporary states, not permanent truths. Many people find that with the right support and tools, their relationship with even the most difficult emotions can shift meaningfully over time.
