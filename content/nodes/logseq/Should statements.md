- "Should statements" are a common thinking pattern that many people experience without realizing it. They involve telling yourself — or others — that things must, ought to, or should be a certain way. While these thoughts can feel like helpful motivators, they often create unnecessary pressure and contribute to feelings of [[Shame and guilt|guilt]], frustration, or inadequacy when reality doesn't match the internal rulebook.
- This type of thinking is recognized in [[Cognitive behavioral therapy|cognitive behavioral therapy]] (CBT) — a well-researched approach to understanding how thoughts influence emotions and behavior — as a cognitive distortion, meaning a habitual way of thinking that can skew how we see ourselves and the world. Common examples include thoughts like "I should be further along by now," "I shouldn't feel this way," or "I should always be there for everyone." Over time, these internal demands can quietly erode [[Self-compassion|self-compassion]] and make everyday challenges feel more overwhelming than they need to be.
- One approach many people find helpful is noticing when "should" language appears in their inner dialogue and gently questioning it. Asking yourself "according to whom?" or "is this expectation realistic?" can create a small but meaningful pause between the thought and its emotional impact. Another strategy involves replacing "should" with more flexible language, such as "I'd like to" or "it would help if." This shift doesn't lower your standards — it simply makes room for being human. Some people also find it useful to explore where certain "should" rules came from, whether family expectations, cultural messages, or past experiences, since understanding the origin of a belief can reduce its grip.
- Recognizing unhelpful thought patterns is a skill that takes practice, and nobody has to work through it alone. Many people find that speaking with a mental health professional can offer valuable support in identifying and gently reshaping the inner voice. Reaching out is a meaningful step, and things can genuinely change with time and the right support.
