- Rumination — the experience of thoughts looping repeatedly through your mind, often replaying worries, regrets, or upsetting events — is something many people recognise. While it can feel like problem-solving, rumination rarely leads to new insights or solutions. Instead, it tends to keep distress alive and can make it harder to move forward. Understanding what rumination is, and knowing that it can be interrupted, is an encouraging first step.
- One strategy many people find helpful is gently redirecting attention toward the present moment. When thoughts begin to spiral, grounding techniques — such as noticing five things you can see, or focusing on the sensation of your feet on the floor — can create a small but meaningful break in the cycle. This isn't about forcing thoughts away, but about giving your mind somewhere else to rest, even briefly.
- Another approach is behavioural engagement: deliberately moving into an activity that absorbs your attention. This might be physical movement, a creative task, or a conversation with someone you trust. Research suggests that activities requiring genuine concentration are particularly effective, because they occupy the mental space that rumination tends to fill. Even a short walk or a brief creative task can shift the pattern.
- It can also help to notice when rumination tends to occur — perhaps in quiet moments, before sleep, or during routine tasks — and to gently plan for those times. This kind of self-awareness builds over time and can make the habit feel less automatic and more manageable.
- Rumination is a very common human experience, and struggling with it does not reflect a personal failing. Many people find that working with a mental health professional helps them understand their patterns more deeply and develop strategies that suit their individual needs. Support is available, and things can change.
