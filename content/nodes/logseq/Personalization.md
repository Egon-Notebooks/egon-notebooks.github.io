- Personalization is a common thinking pattern in which a person attributes external events — things that happen around them or to others — as being directly caused by or related to themselves. It can feel very convincing in the moment, but understanding it can be an important step toward feeling less burdened by events outside of our control.
- When personalization takes hold, everyday situations can become sources of self-blame or [[Shame and guilt|shame]]. A friend cancels plans, and the immediate thought is "they must be upset with me." A colleague seems quiet in a meeting, and the inner voice whispers "I must have done something wrong." These interpretations feel automatic and certain, yet they rarely reflect the full picture. Most situations have many possible explanations, and the one that centers on personal fault is often the least likely.
- One approach many people find helpful is gently questioning the automatic thought. When a self-blaming interpretation arises, it can be useful to ask: "What are some other reasons this might have happened?" Listing even two or three alternative explanations can create some mental breathing room. This technique, often used in [[Cognitive behavioral therapy|cognitive behavioral therapy]] (CBT), helps train the mind to consider a wider view rather than defaulting to self-criticism.
- Another strategy involves noticing the pattern itself. Simply becoming aware that personalization is happening — naming it, even quietly — can create a small but meaningful distance between the thought and the feeling it triggers. Some people find it helpful to keep a brief journal of moments when the pattern appears, which over time can reveal just how often situations have nothing to do with them at all.
- It is completely normal to struggle with thought patterns like personalization, and many people do. With time, support, and the right tools, these patterns can shift. Speaking with a mental health professional can make a real difference in learning to relate to difficult thoughts with greater kindness and perspective.
