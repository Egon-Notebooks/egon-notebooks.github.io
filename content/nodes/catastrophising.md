- Catastrophising is a thinking pattern where the mind jumps to the worst possible outcome of a situation, often far beyond what the evidence suggests is likely. It is a very common experience — many people find themselves spiralling into "what if the worst happens?" thinking, particularly during stressful periods. Understanding this pattern is the first step toward loosening its grip.
- One approach many people find helpful is gently questioning the thoughts themselves. When a catastrophic thought arises, it can be useful to ask: "What is the evidence for this outcome? What are some other possibilities?" This is not about forcing false positivity, but about widening the lens so the mind can consider a fuller range of outcomes — including realistic and manageable ones. Over time, this practice can help interrupt the automatic nature of catastrophic thinking.
- Another strategy involves grounding attention in the present moment. Catastrophising typically pulls the mind far into an imagined future. Simple grounding techniques — such as focusing on slow, steady breathing, noticing physical sensations, or naming things in the immediate environment — can help interrupt the spiral and bring a sense of calm back to the body and mind. Many people find that even a few minutes of this kind of deliberate refocusing makes a noticeable difference.
- It can also be worthwhile to notice patterns — certain situations, times of day, or states of tiredness that seem to trigger catastrophic thinking. Building this self-awareness gently, without self-criticism, can make the pattern feel less overwhelming and more something that can be worked with.
- Catastrophising is not a character flaw or a sign that something is deeply wrong. It is a habit of mind that can shift with time, practice, and the right support. If these thought patterns are significantly affecting daily life, speaking with a mental health professional can be a meaningful and helpful step.
